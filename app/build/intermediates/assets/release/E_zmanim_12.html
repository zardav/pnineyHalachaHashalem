Chapter Twelve: Lighting the Chanukah Candles

12.1 – The Mitzvah to Light Chanukah Candles

Our Sages instituted [a mitzvah] to light candles all eight days of Chanukah, when the Jewish people celebrated and gave thanks to God for helping them defeat the Greeks, liberate Jerusalem, and purify the Holy Temple. In addition, the oil in the Temple’s menorah burned miraculously during those days.

Even though lighting Chanukah candles is a rabbinic mitzvah, we recite a blessing over it: “Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, Who has sanctified us with His commandments and has commanded us to light the Chanukah candle.” One could seemingly ask, how can we say “He has commanded us,” when the Written Law does not contain such a commandment? [The answer is]: the Torah gives the Sages the authority to institute mitzvot, within the guidelines of the Torah, as it says, According to the torah that they will teach you and according to the judgment that they will say to you, shall you do; you shall not deviate from what they will tell you, right or left (Devarim 17:11). It also says, Remember the days of old, consider the years of generation after generation; ask your father and he will tell you; your elders, and they will say to you (ibid. 32:7) (Shabbat 23a). Thus, in order to remember and publicize the miracle that HaShem performed on our behalf during the Second Temple era, the Sages instituted [a mitzvah] to light candles all eight days of Chanukah.

Women are obligated to keep this mitzvah just as men are. Even though this is a time-bound, positive commandment, from which women are usually exempt, nonetheless, they too must fulfill this mitzvah because women participated in the miracle, as well (Shabbat 23a; however, the custom is for married women to discharge their obligation by way of their husband’s lighting; see below note 2, and above 11.11, note 14).

The purpose of all the laws that the Sages instituted regarding where and when to light Chanukah candles is to publicize the miracle. Therefore, they required that the candles be lit near the door or in front of a window facing the main thoroughfare, so that passersby can see them (as will be explained below, 13.1-3). They also determined that the candles be lit after sunset, when the maximum number of people will see them. After all, darkness has already [begun to] descend, making the lights recognizable, and at the same time the streets are still filled with people coming home from work (we will elaborate below, 13.4). Publicizing the miracle, however, is not a necessary prerequisite for the fulfillment of the mitzvah. Even a Jew who lives alone on a deserted island must light the candles, in order to remind himself of the miracle.

This mitzvah is exceedingly great; even a poor man who cannot afford to buy candles must seek donations or sell his shirt in order to buy Chanukah candles. [The halachah] does not demand of one to go begging or to sell his shirt for the sake of other mitzvot (see Rama,O.C. 656:1), but since this mitzvah publicizes the miracle [of Chanukah], its obligation is greater. A poor man need not fulfill the mitzvah in the most elaborate way; it is sufficient for him to light one candle each night (S.A. 671:1, A.H.S. 671:2).

12.2 – The Number of Candles and the Custom of Mehadrin Min HaMehadrin

The mitzvah of lighting Chanukah candles is a very beloved one. In general, there are two levels of mitzvah performance: carrying out the basic obligation and mehadrin, fulfilling the mitzvah more elaborately. When it comes to Chanukah candles, however, there are three levels: the basic obligation, mehadrin, and mehadrin min ha’mehadrin, the best possible way. Furthermore, all of Israel has adopted the practice to fulfill this mitzvah according to the mehadrin min ha’mehadrin custom.

Minimally, every household has to light one candle every night of Chanukah, no matter how many people live in the house; and this candle enables them to remember and publicize the miracle of Chanukah. Those who are mehadrin (more meticulous) light one candle for every adult member of the household. For example, if four [adults] live in the house, they light [all together] four candles every night of Chanukah, demonstrating that everyone has a share in the mitzvah.

Those who are mehadrin min ha’mehadrin (extra meticulous) light a different number of candles each night of Chanukah, and the Rabbis dispute the matter. The scholars of Shammai’s academy (Beit Shammai) say that we light eight candles on the first night and subtract one candle every subsequent night, eventually lighting one candle on the last night. According to this viewpoint, the number of candles corresponds to the number of days yet to come. On the first night, we light eight candles because there are eight more days to Chanukah, and on the last night we light one candle because there is only one day left. The scholars of Hillel’s academy (Beit Hillel) say that we light one candle on the first night and add a candle every subsequent night, eventually lighting eight candles on the last night. According to them, the number of candles corresponds to the number of days that have already passed – that is, every night we light candles according to the number of days the miracle lasted. This way, we demonstrate the magnitude of the miracle, because every additional day that [the Hasmoneans] lit the Menorah in the Temple using the same oil flask, the miracle increased. In addition, this method allows us to ascend in holiness, until we reach the pinnacle on the eighth night when we light eight candles (Shabbat 21b). All of Israel has accepted the mehadrin min ha’mehadrin custom, according to Beit Hillel’s interpretation (S.A. O.C. 671:2).

What emerges, in practice, is that we light thirty-six candles over the course of the eight days of Chanukah. The [prevalent] custom, though, is to light an additional candle every night, to serve as a shamash (lit., attendant), so that one can use its light, if needed. After all, it is forbidden to derive benefit from the Chanukah candles. The shamash, however, is separated from the other candles, because the main objective of mehadrin min ha’mehadrin is to make the number of candles recognizable, for that demonstrates the magnification of the miracle (we will elaborate below, halachah 10-11).

12.3 – The Sefardic Custom

There are different customs regarding the actual fulfillment of mehadrin min ha’mehadrin. According to Sefardic tradition, the main hiddur (adornment) is to light as many candles as corresponds to the day [of Chanukah that one is currently celebrating]. Even if there are many people living in the house, only one of them lights Chanukah candles, based on the number of days that have already passed [in the holiday]. [That is], he lights one on the first night, two on the second, and eight on the eighth. After all, the goal is to show how many days the miracle lasted, for this publicizes the miracle more. And if everyone in the house would light candles within a tefach (handbreadth) of the doorway, one would not be able to recognize which day it is, because everyone’s candles would conjoin and confuse the tally. Since [according to this custom] only one person lights, it is fitting for the head of the household to light on behalf of everyone else.

If the children ask to light candles, [their parents] can let them light their own Chanukah menorahs, as long as they are careful to make a separation between the menorahs. According to the custom of most Sefardim, [these children] do not recite a blessing [when they light], for that would constitute an unnecessary blessing. Rabbi Meshash, however, rules that if they want to merit reciting a blessing, they should have in mind not to fulfill their obligation with their fathers’ lighting. Then, they may be meticulous and light the candles with a blessing (Yalkut Shemesh, O.C. 192).

12.4 – The Ashkenazi Custom: Men, Women, and Children

According to Ashkenazi custom, all members of a household must light their own menorahs in order to fulfill mehadrin min ha’mehadrin. [That is], on the first night everyone lights one candle and on the eighth night everyone lights eight. We are not concerned that onlookers will not know how many candles are being lit on any given night, because we are careful to separate the menorahs from one another.

Children who have reached the age of chinuch (education) light the candles with a blessing. The age of chinuch begins at around six years old, which is when children understand the story of the miracle and the commandment [to light].

The [prevalent] custom among married women is not to light Chanukah candles, because when their husbands light it is considered as if they have lit, for “one’s wife is as one’s own body.” In many homes, young women and girls who have reached the age of chinuch also follow this custom of not lighting the candles. Nevertheless, they may light with a blessing if they so wish. Seemingly, it is good to encourage young girls who have reached the age of chinuch to light the candles, at least until they become bat-mitzvah (12), for the lighting connects them to Torah and mitzvot. And if they wish to continue lighting afterwards, they should be blessed. Even a woman whose husband lights candles may light her own menorah, with a blessing, if she so wishes, despite the custom.

12.5 – The Blessings and HaNeirot Halalu

Our Sages prescribed that we say two blessings before lighting the Chanukah candles, so that we focus on the two aspects of the mitzvah. The first blessing relates to the mitzvah itself and its formula is as follows: “Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, Who has sanctified us with His commandments and has commanded us to light the candle of Chanukah” (להדליק נר של חנוכה). Sefardic Jews conclude with, “to light the Chanukah candle” (להדליק נר חנוכה). The second blessing expresses our thanks to God for the miracles He performed on behalf of our forefathers at the time of Chanukah. The Rabbis enacted that we say it when we light the candles because the candles are supposed to remind us of the miracles and their meaning. Its formulation is: “Blessed are You, Lord our God, King of the universe, Who has done miracles for our forefathers, in those days, during this time.” On the first day, we add the She’hechiyanu blessing, which expresses our thanks to God for keeping us alive and sustaining us for another year, giving us the privilege to once again reach the days of Chanukah and fulfill the mitzvah of lighting the candles.

Immediately following the blessings, one begins lighting the candles, without talking in-between. After the first candle is lit, the custom is to start saying HaNeirot Halalu (which originates in Tractate Sofrim 20:6). Then, while reciting this paragraph, one continues lighting the remaining candles. And even though more candles still need to be lit, [reciting HaNeirot Halalu] does not constitute an interruption. After all, we already learned that the minimal obligation of the mitzvah is fulfilled by lighting the first candle, while the purpose of the remaining candles is to perform the mitzvah more elaborately (hidur mitzvah). And even though one should, ideally, refrain from speaking until after all the candles are lit, HaNeirot Halalu [is different]. Since it explains the purpose of the mitzvah, there is room to say that one should recite it specifically while fulfilling the mitzvah. However, one who finds it difficult to say HaNeirot Halalu while lighting the candles may say it afterwards (see M.B. 676:8, Mishbatzot Zahav 5).

Some people recite LeShem Yichud prior to the blessings and HaNeirot Halalu, in order to supplement the mitzvah with [special] intentions.

One should light each candle well and wait until the flame takes firm hold onto the majority of the wick, not like those who are in a rush and move onto the next candle before the previous one is properly lit (B.H. 673:2, s.v. hadlakah).

12.6 – Family Participation in the Mitzvah

One should try to gather the entire family for candle lighting, so that everyone can hear the blessings, answer “Amen,” and witness the lighting. Besides the fact that this glorifies the mitzvah and publicizes the miracle, it is necessary for those who are not reciting the blessings themselves, like women and children, who fulfill their obligation with the lighting of their husband/father. By hearing the blessings, they take part in thanking God for the miracles. According to the Rambam and Rashi, if the family members do not hear the blessings, they must look at the candles and recite the blessing “Who has done miracles,” despite the fact that they have discharged their obligation of lighting the candles with the head of the household’s lighting. The Rashba and the Ran, however, maintain that since these family members have fulfilled the mitzvah of lighting, even though they did not hear the blessings, they do not have to recite “Who has done miracles” upon seeing the candles. Since the matter is under dispute, one should not recite the blessing ( S.A. 676:3). Ideally (le-chatchilah), though, one who is not lighting and saying the blessings himself should hear them [from someone else] and answer “Amen,” in order to fulfill the mitzvah according to all poskim.

Therefore, anyone who discharges his or her obligation by way of someone else’s lighting, like women or children whose husbands/fathers light for them, must take part in the candle lighting ceremony, so that they can hear the blessings and answer “Amen.” And even if they cannot be home for the lighting, they should try to be in a different house or in the synagogue when the candles are being lit, thereby fulfilling their obligation according to all opinions.

12.7 – The Candles

All types of oils and wicks are kosher for Chanukah candles, even those that are unusable for Shabbat candles. After all, the purpose of Shabbat candles is to illuminate the house, and if they do not light well, there is concern that one may tilt a candle to improve its light and thus desecrate the Sabbath. Therefore, the Sages prohibited lighting Shabbat candles with oils and wicks that do not light well. On the other hand, one is not allowed to use the light of the Chanukah candles. Therefore, any type of oil or wick that is capable of staying lit for half-an-hour is kosher for Chanukah candles.

The nicer the candle burns, the more ornamental (mehudar) it is, because the miracle is publicized better this way. Therefore, many people are accustomed to lighting wax or paraffin candles, whose flame is strong and nice. Many Acharonim write that it is even better to light with olive oil, because its light is lucid, in addition to the fact that it reminds one of the miracle of the oil flask.

The Chanukah candles must contain enough fuel to last for thirty minutes, because the Rabbis prescribed that we light from the end of sunset until people stop walking around the marketplace, which is around half-an-hour. And even if someone lights indoors, the candles have to be able to last for thirty minutes. One who has a small amount of oil or a small candle, which will only burn a few minutes, should light without reciting a blessing.

12.8 – Electric Lights

After electricity was discovered, the question was raised: can one fulfill the mitzvah of lighting Chanukah candles with electric lights?

In practice, most poskim hold that one cannot use electric lights, because they do not have wicks and oil like [conventional] candles do. Furthermore, since they emit a very strong light, they may be considered like torches, as opposed to candles. Our master, Rav Kook zt”l writes that since electricity did not exist when the Sages instituted the mitzvah, it is not considered one of the types of candles that are included in the Rabbinic enactment by which one can fulfill the mitzvah (Mitzvat Re’iyah, O.C. 673).

It is true that when it comes to Shabbat candles, most authorities hold that – when necessary – one can fulfill the mitzvah, with a blessing, by way of electric lights, because the main purpose of Shabbat candles is to provide light. Chanukah candles, however, are meant to remind us of the miracle. Therefore, they must resemble the candles of the Holy Temple, and since electric lights are different from candles, one cannot discharge his obligation by lighting them.

If one does not have a kosher candle, however, he may – be’di’avad – light an electric bulb, without reciting a blessing. By doing so, he affirms the miracle and, according to a few poskim, even fulfills the mitzvah.

Some have a custom to place large, electric Chanukah menorahs, whose light can be seen from afar, in public areas. Even though this does not fulfill the Rabbinic commandment [to light Chanukah candles], there is, nonetheless, virtue in doing so, for it reminds the masses of the miracle of Chanukah.

12.9 – Lighting is the Mitzvah

The mitzvah is fulfilled by lighting the candles, not by having them lit. This is evident from the formulation of the blessing: “Who has sanctified us with His commandments and has commanded us to light the Chanukah candle.” Therefore, if one lights candles that can burn for half-an-hour and someone knocks them down by accident, causing them to go out before thirty minutes have passed, he need not relight them, because he fulfilled the mitzvah at the time of lighting. Even if one lights inferior candles, such that there is some concern that they might go out, he has discharged his obligation, assuming that they usually last for thirty minutes. Nonetheless, the Acharonim rule that it is fitting to act strictly and relight the candles, so that they may light the half-hour period that the Sages prescribed (S.A. 673:2, M.B. 27).

However, if one lights the candles in a place where they cannot burn for half-an-hour, like in a windy area, and the wind actually blows them out before they manage to burn for thirty minutes, he has not fulfilled his obligation, because they were not fitting to last the required amount of time from the moment he lit them. Most authorities maintain that [in such a situation] one should relight the candles with a blessing. In practice, however, one should relight them without a blessing, because the issue is unclear, and the rule is “We are lenient when doubt arises regarding blessings.”

As previously stated, the mitzvah is fulfilled by lighting the candles, not by placing them in their proper place. Therefore, if someone lights a candle prior to nightfall and puts it down in a place that is valid for Chanukah candles, he has not fulfilled his obligation, since it was not lit for the sake of the mitzvah. And even if he lifts it up while it’s lit and then places it down with intention to perform the mitzvah, he has not discharged his obligation. Rather, he must extinguish it and relight it for the sake of the mitzvah. Then, there is no need to lift the menorah up and place it down for the sake of the mitzvah (Shabbat 23a, S.A. 675:1).

One must light the candles where they are supposed to be set down. Thus, even if the head of the household is ill and cannot get out of bed, one may not bring him candles in order for him to light by his bedside, and then transfer them to their proper place. Rather, the head of the household should recite the blessings while someone else lights for him where the candles are supposed to be (B.I.Ch., VaYeishev 6). If one puts the candles on the windowsill, but forgets to move them close to the window before lighting, he may move them a bit after the lighting, so that the passersby can see them better.

12.10 – The Prohibition of Benefitting from the Candles

It is forbidden to benefit from the light of the Chanukah candles, whether for mundane purposes, like counting money, or sacred ones, like studying [Torah]. The reason being that the candles are designated for the mitzvah of Chanukah, and just as it is forbidden to benefit from the candles of the Temple menorah, so too, it is forbidden to benefit from the Chanukah candles, which were instituted in commemoration of the miracle that occurred in the Temple menorah. Furthermore, the purpose of the candles is to publicize the miracle, and if one were to use their light, it would appear as if he lit them in order to illuminate [the room], not in order to publicize the miracle (S.A. 673:1).

It is forbidden to use left-over oil or candles that were not completely consumed, because they were allocated for the mitzvah the moment they were lit. Preferably, one should use them for future Chanukah candles, but if they are not fitting for such use, or if they are left over after the eighth night, one should make a bonfire and burn them (S.A. 677:4, M.B. 18). Alternatively, one can pour the extra oil down the drain and put the remaining wicks in the garbage. Candles left in the package and oil left in the bottle may be used for any purpose.

If one begins lighting the candles and the shamash goes out, he may not relight it using one of the mitzvah candles, because the shamash is unsanctified and one may not light a mundane candle from a mitzvah candle. If, however, one has used up all his matches, and if he does not relight the shamash he will not be able to continue lighting the rest, he should light it from a Chanukah candle.

According to the letter of the law, one may benefit from the candles after they have burned for half-an-hour. However, the custom is to act strictly and refrain from deriving any pleasure from them, even after thirty minutes pass, because the lights still publicize the miracle even then, and if one were to use them, it would seem as if he is defaming the mitzvah. Furthermore, since he prepared the candles for the sake of a mitzvah and did not plan on benefitting from them after half-an-hour, some say that the entire candle [or cup of oil] has been sanctified for the mitzvah, making it forbidden to use it for mundane purposes.

12.11 – The Chanukah Menorah and the Shamash

It is fitting to glorify [the mitzvah] and light the candles in a nice menorah, each person according to his means. Some even buy menorahs made of gold or silver. One who does not have a Chanukah menorah may stick candles onto a flat surface and light them [just like that]. Similarly, one may take small glass cups, fill them with oil and wicks, and light them. One should not, however, light the candles in disgraceful vessels, like soot-filled earthenware ( S.A. 673:3, M.B. 28, K.H.C. 60-62).

One should be careful to leave space between the candles, so that each one can be seen separately, thus publicizing the miracle. In addition, if the candles are too close to one another, their flames may join together like a torch, which disqualifies them. Regarding wax candles, another concern arises: if they are too close, they will cause each other to melt before thirty minutes pass (S.A. 671:4, with commentaries).

Technically speaking, one fulfills the mitzvah even if he does not place the candles at the same height or in a straight line, as long as they are separate and a person standing close by can tell that they correspond to the days of Chanukah. Similarly, one fulfills the mitzvah by placing candlesticks in a circle, seeing that each candle stands alone. Preferably, though, one should place them in a straight line, so that all onlookers can see the candles as one and discern their number, corresponding to the miracle (Rama 671:4, B.H. s.v. u-mutar).

In order to prevent people from violating the prohibition of benefitting from the Chanukah candles, the custom developed to light an extra candle, to serve as a shamash (“attendant”). This way, if one needs light where the candles are burning, he will use the light of the shamash. According to the letter of the law, one need not light a shamash in a room that has electric lights. Nonetheless, many people light a shamash, in order to emphasize the difference between mitzvah candles, whose light we are forbidden to use, and the shamash, whose light we are permitted to use.

However, in order to avoid miscounting the candles, we place the shamash at a different height or a distance away from the other candles, to make it clear to all which are the [main] candles and which one is the shamash. The prevalent custom is to place it higher than the rest, so that if someone needs light, it will be clear that he is using the shamash’s light and not that of the other candles (S.A. and Rama 673:1, M.B. 20).

12.12 – Setting up the Candles and Lighting them

When approaching the task of setting up the menorah, one faces a dilemma: Where should he place the candle on the first night? Where should he place the second candle on the second night? And from which candle is it preferable to begin lighting? Even though one fulfills the mitzvah, mehadrin min ha-mehadrin, no matter how he lights, the ideal practice is as follows: On the first night, one places the candle on the right side of the menorah (from the perspective of the lighter, who is facing the menorah), because the right side always takes precedence over the left. On the second night, one adds an additional candle to the left [of the first one], and after he recites the blessings, he lights the additional candle first, followed by the one to its right. There are two reasons for this: 1) It is preferable to begin with the new candle, which symbolizes the intensification of the miracle. 2) After lighting the leftmost candle, the lighter will have to turn to his right in order to light the next candle, and our Sages teach ( Yoma 15b) that one should always turn rightward.

On each subsequent night, one adds a new candle to the left [of the ones he lit on the previous nights] and lights it first. Then, he turns rightward and lights the rest. When reciting the blessings, it is proper to stand to the left of the menorah so that the closest candle will be the one he lights first. This way, a person avoids skipping over the other candles in order to light this one (S.A. 676:5, M.B. 11).

12.13 – Prior to the Lighting

One is forbidden to eat a half-hour before candle-lighting time, which is when the stars emerge (as we will explain below, 13.4). One may not even begin a light meal, for fear that he may prolong the meal and forget to light. It is also forbidden to drink alcoholic beverages, but one may eat fruits and vegetables. One may even eat bread or mezonot foods, as long as he eats less than an egg-size (around 56 grams). [Besides eating], one may not begin, during that half-hour period, any type of work that may drag on. Nor should one go to sleep during that time. One may begin to eat, work, or sleep during this period if he asks a friend to remind him to light on time. Nevertheless, even if one begins these things in a permissible fashion – i.e., more than thirty minutes before candle lighting time – he must stop what he is doing when the stars emerge and light the candles, so as not to miss the time that the Sages enacted (M.B. 672:10; Sha’ar HaTziyun, ibid.; Peninei Halachah, Tefillah 25:9).

One who is in the middle of work and cannot get home in time to light at tzeit ha-kochavim (when the stars emerge) may continue working until he is finished. However, he must be careful not to eat until he fulfills the mitzvah. And if his work is the kind that can drag on for a long time, to the point where there is concern that he will end up forgetting to light the candles, he may continue working only if he asks a friend to remind him to light when his work is done (see below, 13.6, 13.9).

It is also forbidden to study Torah once the time for candle-lighting arrives. However, if a regular shiur (Torah lesson) takes place at that time, and it would be difficult to reschedule it for another time, it is best to go through with the shiur and remind everyone at its conclusion to light the candles (see below, 13.6, n. 13).

12.14 – Lighting in the Synagogue

The custom is to light Chanukah candles in the synagogue; and we recite there all of the blessings that we recite at home. According to Rabbinic law, one needs to light only at home. Nonetheless, the custom developed to light in the synagogue, as well, in order to publicize the miracle even more. The candles are customarily placed [near] the southern wall of the synagogue, similar to the location of the menorah in the Holy Temple. One does not discharge his obligation by way of this lighting, and even the person who actually recites the blessings and lights the candles in the synagogue must relight, with the blessings, at home (S.A. 671:7).

The custom is to light the candles between Mincha and Ma’ariv, because that is when the largest number of people are in the synagogue and the miracle will be publicized most. After Ma’ariv, however, people are in a rush to get home and light.

In synagogues where they pray Ma’ariv late, significantly after the stars emerge, they should light the candles before Ma’ariv.

Most congregations pray Mincha on Friday afternoon shortly before sunset, making it impossible to light [Chanukah] candles after Mincha, because that is when one has to usher in the Sabbath. Therefore, people [in such communities] should light before Mincha. Congregations that pray Mincha a long time before sunset, however, should light the candles after Mincha and make sure to finish lighting a long time before sunset, so that they can take in the Sabbath before sunset and fulfill the mitzvah of adding from the mundane [weekday] onto the holy [Shabbat] ( M.B. 671:47). On Saturday night, we light the candles [in synagogue] after Ma’ariv, because we do not end Shabbat beforehand.

Since the reason for lighting candles in the synagogue is to publicize the miracle, at least ten people must be present at the time of lighting. What should one do if ten people have yet to assemble, and it is not possible to delay the lighting – on Friday, for example, when there is concern that the congregation will not manage to finish praying Mincha on time? Some say that they may recite the blessings and light the candles even though less than ten people will hear the blessings, because ten people will [certainly] gather together afterwards and see the candles (M.A., M.B. 671:47, B.H. ibid.). Others maintain that they should light without a blessing (Mor U’Ketzi’ah, K.H.C. 671:72).

12.15 – Candle Lighting at Public Gatherings

Many people are scrupulous to publicize the miracle and light Chanukah candles wherever people gather, like weddings, bar mitzvahs, bat mitzvahs, Chanukah parties, and lectures. The question is: is it permissible to recite a blessing over the lighting at such events?

Many contemporary rabbis hold that one should not recite a blessing, because the blessings are customarily said only in synagogues, and we do not have the authority to invent new customs in other places. According to them, one who recites a blessing in places other than a synagogue is pronouncing a blessing in vein. The reason we light specifically in synagogues is probably to commemorate the menorah that was lit in the Holy Temple, synagogues being considered miniature Temples. In other places, however, it is inappropriate to light with a blessing.

Nevertheless, several poskim maintain that one may light Chanukah candles, with a blessing, wherever there is a public gathering. After all, the reason we light in the synagogue is to publicize the miracle; therefore, one should light, with a blessing, wherever the masses gather. It is preferable, though, to pray Mincha and Ma’ariv – or even just Ma’ariv – in such a place, thus giving it the status of a synagogue, to some degree. Then, a blessing may be recited, as the custom dictates.

In practice, one who wishes to rely on those who hold that it is permissible to light with a blessing may do so. And if the guests at the event include secular Jews, who may not have lit candles at home, it is very important to light with a blessing, because only then will everyone stand up – to hear the blessings – causing the miracle to be publicized in front of their eyes. They will also learn how to fulfill the mitzvah [properly]. If possible, it is preferable to ask someone who is unaccustomed to performing mitzvot to recite the blessings and light the candles. This way, he will become closer to faith, Torah, and mitzvot.


[1] On a simple level, if the custom of mehadrin is to light one candle per member of the household [every night], then the mehadrin min ha’mehadrin custom requires one to light an additional candle per night for everyone in the house. This is the Rambam’s opinion (Hilchot Chanukah 4:1-3), as well as the Ashkenazi custom. According to Rabbeinu Yitzchak (Tosafot, Shabbat 21b), however, only one member of the house lights a menorah, for if everyone lights, [onlookers] will not know what day of Chanukah is being celebrated, and the main objective of mehadrin min ha’mehadrin is to publicize the miracle based on the number of days [it lasted]. Sefardim follow this custom, as written in the Shulchan Aruch (671:22). (The Re’ah explains that doing it this way glorifies the miracle more [than the mehadrin custom does], because most households do not have that many members. Therefore, lighting [one menorah and adding a candle every night] amounts to more candles. Furthermore, even if fewer candles will be lit this way, it is preferable that people will have to clarify which day of Chanukah it is.) Many authorities explain that the difference between the customs is based on where people used to light. Sefardim lit their candles near the entranceway; therefore, if many members of the house would light there, people wouldn’t know which day of Chanukah it is. Ashkenazim, on the other hand, were accustomed to lighting inside the house, so everyone is able to light their own menorah. The author of Darkei Moshe (671:1) thus writes in the name of R. Avraham of Prague that, according to the mehadrin min ha’mehadrin custom, everyone in the house must light his or her own menorah when lighting indoors, even according to Tosafot. See Torat HaMo’adim 1:4.

The Vilna Gaon writes in Bi’ur HaGra that the main reason the Gemara gives for Beit Hillel’s opinion is: “We ascend in holiness,” and one need not know what day of Chanukah it is. Rather, the main goal is to increase the number of candles. Thus, the Gra dismisses Tosafot’s reasoning, indicating that even when everyone lights at the entranceway, they should light according to the number of people in the house and the number of days [that have passed in the holiday].

Some infer from the words of the Rambam that one person should light for everyone, but according to Ashkenazi custom, based on the Maharil, everyone lights their own candles and recites their own blessings. Rabbi Rabinowitz writes in Melumdei Milchamah (p. 232) that according to Rabbi Kapach’s version [of the Rambam], Ashkenazi custom is the same as the Rambam’s opinion. The Taz (677:1) and Magen Avraham (677:9) explain that since the members of the house have no intention of fulfilling their obligation with the head of the household’s blessings, they may recite the blessings over their candles. This implies that they would not be able to recite the blessings otherwise, because one does not make a blessing over a hiddur (an adornment of a mitzvah). The author of Sefat Emet (Shabbat 21b), however, postulates that from the very beginning, [the Rabbis] determined that extra meticulous people (mehadrin min ha’mehadrin) should recite the blessings, even though they have already fulfilled their minimal obligation. Another dispute involves one who lights a single candle, with a blessing, and later obtains enough candles to fulfill the mitzvah more elaborately (mehadrin). Should he recite a blessing over the lighting of the extra candles or not? Eliyah Rabbah holds that he recites a blessing, but according to the Pri Chadash (end of 672), no blessing is said; and Rabbi Akiva Eiger ( Responsa, Tanina 13; study it carefully) seems to lean towards that opinion, based on the implication [made above] from the words of the Taz and Magen Avraham.

Does the lighting of the head of a household absolve his family members of their obligation if they planned on lighting by themselves but ended up not doing so? This issue needs clarification. In my humble opinion, they discharge their obligation, be’di’avad, because the minimal requirement is to have one candle in the house, and there was. Therefore, whether they like it or not, the family members have fulfilled the mitzvah unadornedly. The fact that they intended not to discharge their obligation, in accordance with the words of the Taz and Magen Avraham, that relates only to the hiddur, i.e., that they will light with a blessing. Regarding the lighting itself, however, they are exempted [by the head of the household’s lighting]. The matter still needs further study. The halachah is the same according to the Sefat Emet’s rationale. One could also decide leniently based on the rule that bereirah (retrospective designation) applies in cases of Rabbinic law.

[5] The Talmud states in Shabbat 23a, “One who sees the Chanukah candles must recite a blessing.” [The Gemara] explains that one who lights recites two blessings, “To light” and “Who has done miracles,” while one who [merely] sees [candles] recites one blessing – “Who has done miracles.” On the first night, the She’hechiyanu blessing is added. [The commentators] disagree on what kind of “seer” the Gemara refers to. According to Rashi, Rambam, and Mordechai, one whose relative lights on his behalf, but he fails to hear the blessings, recites the “Who has done miracles” blessing upon seeing the candles. This is so because the mitzvah has two components: 1) to light Chanukah candles at home, in order to publicize the miracle; 2) to thank HaShem for the miracle by seeing the candles. One whose relative lights on his behalf at home has fulfilled the mitzvah of lighting, but since he did not hear the “Who has done miracles” blessing, he has not fulfilled the mitzvah of giving thanks. Therefore, he recites the blessing upon seeing Chanukah candles. For example, a woman who did not hear her husband say the blessings when he lit must recite “Who has done miracles” when she comes [home] later and sees the candles. Similarly, a household member who was absent when the candles were lit recites the blessing of “Who has done miracles” when he walks through the streets and sees Chanukah candles in the window of someone’s house. On the other hand, Rashba, Ran, and Smag hold that only one who has not discharged his obligation – because he has no one to light for him – recites “Who has done miracles” upon seeing Chanukah candles.

Therefore, he must gather his family together shortly before lighting the candles. This [ruling] is found in M.A. (672:5), Ch.A. (154:20), M.B. (672:10), B.I.Ch. (VaYeishev 1), and others.
