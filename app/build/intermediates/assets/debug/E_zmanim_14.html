Chapter Fourteen: The Month of Adar

14.1 – When Adar Arrives We Increase Joy

Our Sages state, “When Av arrives we decrease joy, and when Adar arrives we increase joy” (Ta’anit 29a). By this, the Rabbis wanted to teach us that nothing happens by chance and that every time period has its own character and nature. If both Temples were destroyed on the ninth of Av, it is a sign that the beginning of Av is naturally predisposed to calamity. And if the miracle of Purim occurred in the middle of the month of Adar, it is a sign that this month has the ability to transform bad into good. We feel ordinary joy over the good that exists in the world, but our joy is not complete, because there is still evil and pain in the world. However, when even the bad turns into good, our joy is great and complete. This is what happened on Purim. The Holy One, blessed be He, turned the bad into good and saved His nation Israel. This taught us that everything that happens in the world, even the bad, eventually turns around and becomes good. The more we increase our faith [in God] and study Torah, the closer we will bring the [ultimate] redemption, when all evil will turn into good, and joy will abound in the world. Since the month of Adar has the unique character of transforming bad into good, “We increase joy when Adar arrives.”

The Sages further recommend (ibid. 29b) that a Jew who has a legal issue or business deal to settle with a Gentile should evade him during the month of Av, because the Jews have bad mazal (“luck”) during that month. Instead, he should try to make himself available for litigation or a deal during the month of Adar, because that is when Israel’s mazal prevails.

14.2 – The Four Parshiot

Our Sages instituted the reading of four portions of the Torah [during this time of year]: Shekalim, Zachor, Parah, and HaChodesh. We generally read them on the Sabbaths of the month of Adar, except for the first one, which is usually read on the Sabbath before Adar begins.

On these Sabbaths, we take out two Torah scrolls from the ark, reading the regular weekly portion, with seven aliyot, from the first scroll and the special parashah, as maftir, from the second scroll. And since the Haftarah, from the Prophets, has to relate to what was read in maftir, the Haftarot of these weeks deal with a theme connected to the parashah that was read in the second scroll and not to the weekly portion that was read in the first.

On the first Shabbat, we read Parashat Shekalim (Shemot 28:9-15), as a reminder to contribute the yearly half-shekel, from which communal sacrifices are purchased. Beginning with the month of Nissan, all sacrifices must be bought with money from the new collection. Therefore, the Rabbis instituted the reading of Parashat Shekalim a month earlier, to remind every Jew to bring in his donation. Even though the Temple is destroyed today, and we do not have the privilege to offer sacrifices, we still read Parashat Shekalim, in commemoration of what was done in the Temple (see M.B. 685:1, Mikra’ei Kodesh 3).

The second parashah is Zachor (Devarim 25:17-19). This reading fulfills a biblical commandment to remember what the Amalekites did to us. The Rabbis ordained that we read this parashah before Purim in order to juxtapose the mitzvah of remembering Amalek to the holiday in which we celebrate its fulfillment. After all, Haman was a descendent of Amalek, and we eliminated him on Purim.

In Parashat Parah (BeMidbar 19:1-22), the third one, we learn how one purifies himself from ritual impurity in order to enter the Beit HaMikdash and offer sacrifices. The Rabbis instituted that we read this in advance of the month of Nissan so that people can prepare and purify themselves for the upcoming Pesach sacrifice. Even though we do not offer this sacrifice nowadays, we read Parashat Parah in commemoration of what was done in the Temple.

The fourth parashah is HaChodesh (Shemot 12:1-20), which mentions the sanctification of the new moon and the mitzvot surrounding Pesach. We read it on the eve of Nissan because Nissan is the first month of the Jewish calendar. In addition, reading this parashah motivates us to start preparing for Passover and all its mitzvot.

When Rosh Chodesh Adar or Nissan coincides with Shabbat, we remove three Torah scrolls from the ark. We read the weekly portion from the first one, the section that deals with Rosh Chodesh [BeMidbar 28:9-15] from the second one, and the special parashah – Shekalim on Rosh Chodesh Adar and HaChodesh on Rosh Chodesh Nissan – from the third scroll.

According to most poskim, the mitzvah of reading Parashat Zachor is biblically ordained. Therefore, people are more careful about its observance than all the other readings, as we will explain below (14.6). Some say that Parashat Parah also fulfills a biblical commandment, which is why people are accustomed to treating it more scrupulously.

14.3 – Leap Years and the First Adar

It is well known that the [Jewish] months are established according to the lunar cycle, while the years follow the solar cycle. The reason for this is that Pesach must always coincide with the spring season, as it says, Observe the month of Aviv (spring) and make a pesach-offering to the Lord your God, for in the month of Aviv (spring) the Lord your God took you out of Egypt at night (Devarim 16:1). In order to keep the months in sync with the solar year, we must establish a leap year every so often. That is, we add a month onto the year, making it thirteen months long. The only month that may be added is the month of Adar, and by doing so we postpone the month of Nissan and cause it to coincide with spring. In the past, a rabbinic court (beit din) would decide to create a leap year based on agricultural concerns and yearly calculations. Today, however, since we do not have [properly] ordained [judges and] courts, the Sages established a fixed cycle of nineteen years, of which twelve are ordinary years and seven are leap years.

On a leap year, we celebrate Purim during the second Adar, in order to juxtapose the joy of the Purim-salvation to the exodus from Egypt. We also read the Four Parshiot during this month because Shekalim, Parah, and HaChodesh were instituted as a preparation for the month of Nissan, and Zachor has to be read [immediately] before Purim, which is celebrated in the second Adar, as well (see Megillah 6b).

Nonetheless, the first Adar has a certain festiveness to it. Therefore, we do not fast, deliver eulogies, or recite Tachanun (supplication prayer) on the fourteenth and fifteenth of the month. It is also proper to eat a special meal on the fourteenth of Adar I, which is called Purim Katan (“the Small Purim”) (Sh.A., Rama 697:1).

Moreover, when the Sages enjoined us to increase joy when Adar arrives, they seemingly meant when the first Adar arrives. (See the footnote regarding the proper time to celebrate a bar mitzvah or observe a yahrtzeit).

14.4 – The Three Mitzvot Concerning the Obliteration of Amalek

Three mitzvot in the Torah relate to Amalek. The first is a positive commandment to remember what Amalek did to us, as it says, Remember what Amalek did to you on the way when you were leaving Egypt (Devarim 25:17). The second is a negative commandment not to forget what Amalek did to us, as it says, Do not forget (ibid. 25:19). The third is a positive commandment to eradicate Amalek’s offspring from the world, as it says, It shall be that when the Lord your God gives you rest from all your enemies around, in the Land that the Lord your God gives you as an inheritance to possess it, you shall obliterate the remembrance of Amalek from under the heavens (ibid.).

The Amalekite people symbolize the root of evil in the world, and they introduced Jew-hatred to mankind. The Jewish people face a difficult struggle in this world. The idealistic, faith-based message that HaShem destined to Israel incites all the evildoers of the world to go out and fight against us. No other nation has been persecuted as much as we have been: from the destruction of the Temple through the Crusades, the Inquisition, the Chmielnicki Uprising of 1648-49, culminating in the horrific Holocaust that ravaged our nation. Amalek started it all.

Right after we left Egypt, even before we had a chance to coalesce and organize ourselves, Amalek came and attacked us, without any provocation or reason. And who did he attack? Slaves who were going free after an extended period of servitude. Amalek is the nation that embodies hatred of Israel, and consequently, hatred of Torah and the godly concept of universal rectification through kindness and truth. This is why the verse says,For the hand [of God] is on the throne (כס) of God (י-ה) , [saying] the Lord will [wage] war against Amalek from generation to generation (Shemot 17:16). Rashi comments, “The Holy One, blessed be He, swore that His name (י-ה-ו-ה) and His throne (כסא) will be incomplete until the name of Amalek is utterly obliterated.”

Jews are naturally kind and compassionate, and many mitzvot in the Torah cultivate such traits within us. We would, therefore, be inclined to forgive Amalek [for his misdeeds], but the Torah commands us to remember what he did and obliterate him. This way, we will remember that there is evil in the world, against which we must fight to the bitter end, without compromise. Only then will we be able to perfect the world.

14.5 – The Mitzvah to Wipe Out Amalek

The mitzvah to destroy Amalek is mainly incumbent upon Klal Yisrael (the Jewish nation as a whole). Thus, our Sages taught that the Jews were commanded to fulfill three mitzvot upon entering Eretz Yisrael: first, to appoint a king over them; afterwards, to wipe out the seed of Amalek; and then, to build the Holy Temple (Sanhedrin 20b).

Indeed, after the Jews merged together in their Land, they appointed King Shaul, and after his kingdom stabilized, the prophet Shmuel approached Shaul and said to him, The Lord sent me to anoint you as king over His people, over Israel; therefore, hear now the voice of the Lord’s words. So says the Lord of Hosts, “I have remembered what Amalek did to Israel, how he set [an ambush] against him on the way, as he [Israel] went up from Egypt. Now go and smite Amalek and destroy everything he has; have no mercy on him; kill both man and woman, infant and suckling, ox and sheep, camel and donkey” (I Shmuel 15:1-3).

However, King Shaul did not fulfill the mitzvah properly, taking pity on Agag, King of Amalek, and the best of the sheep and cattle. As a result, HaShem took the kingdom away from him and gave it to David. Nevertheless, the damage was already done, and it was devastating. Because of Shaul’s weakness and compassion, many Amalekites survived, and they continued harassing Israel. A few years later, a band of Amalekites attacked Tziklag, where the families of David and his men lived, burning down the city and taking all the women and children captive. With God’s help, David and his men managed to rescue the captives and vanquish the marauders. But since David was not yet king and did not have the army of Israel at his disposal, he was unable to eradicate them. Four hundred youths rode on camels and escaped (I Shmuel 30). Apparently, other groups of Amalekites survived elsewhere, but despite his efforts David was unable to battle and destroy them all, even after he became king, because they were spread out far and wide. Chazal also tell us that because Shaul procrastinated in killing Agag, Agag’s seed was preserved – [he impregnated a woman from his prison cell before being killed] – eventually resulting in the birth of Haman the Aggagite, who attempted to wipe out the Jewish people (Megillah 13a).

Even though the mitzvah to eradicate Amalek is mainly incumbent upon the community, every individual Jew is commanded to fulfill it, as well. Therefore, if a Jew meets an Amalekite, and has the ability to kill him, but refrains from doing so, he has neglected this mitzvah ( Sefer HaChinuch 604). The descendants of Amalek are currently unknown, but if one would ascertain that a particular person is an Amalekite, who follows their ways, it would be a mitzvah to kill him.

14.6 – Parashat Zachor

Our Sages instituted the reading of Parashat Zachor once a year in order to fulfill the biblical commandments to remember and not forget the evil deeds of Amalek. One is considered to have forgotten about Amalek only if a year goes by without remembering him. Therefore, we discharge our obligation by mentioning the matter once a year. We read Zachor on the Sabbath before Purim in order to juxtapose the remembering of Amalek to the destruction of his descendent, Haman.

According to biblical law, one must communicate this remembrance verbally. There is no need, however, for every individual to read Parashat Zachor from a Torah scroll; rather, everyone fulfills the mitzvah by hearing the reader chant the verses from the Torah.

According to some of the greatest Rishonim, the Torah commands us to read Parashat Zachor from the Torah scroll itself [as opposed to a printed Chumash]. Therefore, it is advisable to read it from an exceptional Torah scroll, and the reader must try to read it as meticulously as possible.

Preferably, everyone should hear Zachor read according to the melody and pronunciation to which his family is accustomed. From a halachic standpoint, however, members of all the communities may discharge their obligation by hearing it read according to any version accepted among the Jewish people, whether it be Sefardic, Ashkenazic, or Yemenite.

One who finds himself in a place where there is no minyan (a quorum of ten) should read Zachor from a Torah scroll without a minyan. And if no Torah scroll is available, he should read it from a Chumash or a Siddur.

Mitzvot require intent; therefore, one must have intention to fulfill the mitzvah of remembering Amalek’s deeds when reading or hearing Parashat Zachor. It is a good practice for the gabbai (synagogue attendant) or reader to announce this before commencing the reading.

14.7 – Are Women Obligated to Hear Parashat Zachor?

According to most poskim, women are exempt from the mitzvah of remembering Amalek, because this mitzvah is connected to themitzvah of annihilating Amalek, and since women are not commanded to wage war, they need not remember what Amalek did to us ( Sefer HaChinuch 603). Others claim that the mitzvah to wage war applies to women, as well, for they are required to assist the soldiers. Therefore, they, too, are obligated to remember Amalek. And even though the Sages established a fixed time for reading Parashat Zachor – the Sabbath before Purim – it has no time limit according to Torah law. Thus, it is a mitzvah independent of time, and women are obligated to perform it (Minchat Chinuch, ibid.).

Practically speaking, women are exempt from hearing Parashat Zachor. Ideally (le’chatchilah), however, women should hear the reading, and many are accustomed to doing so. A woman who finds it difficult to attend the services, but nevertheless wants to fulfill the mitzvah, should read the parashah herself from a Chumash. After all, many authorities hold that this fulfills the biblical requirement to remember Amalek. If there is a class for women in the synagogue, [a man] may take out a Torah scroll and read Zachor for them. Even though no minyan is present, it is commendable for them to hear the parashah from a kosher Torah scroll.

14.8 – Can an Amalekite Save Himself or Convert to Judaism?

Even though the Torah commands us to wipe out the descendants of Amalek, if one of them agrees to keep the seven Noachide laws, he no longer has the status of an Amalekite, and it is forbidden to kill him. The seven Noachide laws are as follows: the prohibitions against idolatry, adultery, murder, theft, blasphemy, and eating the limbs of a live animal; and the obligation to set up a court system that will adjudicate all interpersonal disputes justly.

Moreover, even if the Amalekites do not volunteer to keep the seven Noachide laws, we are commanded to offer them peace before going to war with them. That is, we offer them the opportunity to adopt the seven Noachide laws and agree to be subservient to the Jewish people, including the payment of tributes. If they accept these conditions of peace, we do not wage war against them. If they refuse, however, we fight them to the finish. Even if they reconsider afterwards and beg for peace, we do not accept them, for once the war has begun we fight them until they are annihilated (Rambam, Hilchot Melachim 6:1-4, with Kesef Mishnah).

The poskim dispute whether or not we accept an Amalekite who wants to convert to Judaism. The Rambam (Hilchot Issurei Bi’ah 12:17) holds that he may convert. Accordingly, Chazal state that descendants of Haman, himself a descendant of Amalek, taught Torah in B’nei Brak ( Gittin 57b, Sanhedrin 96b). Clearly, [our forebears] accepted converts from Amalek.

Others assert that we do not accept Amalekite converts. This is Rabbi Eliezer’s opinion in the Mechilta (end of BeShalach). He relates that HaShem swore by His glorious throne that an Amalekite who comes to convert will not be accepted. And what about Chazal’s statement that Haman’s descendants taught Torah in B’nei Brak? [We must say that] this happened by mistake: a beit din (rabbinic court) converted someone without knowing that he was from Amalek. Alternatively, an Amalekite from the wicked Haman’s lineage raped a Jewish woman, and those Torah teachers from B’nei Brak descended from her son, who was considered a Jew (Resisei Laylah 38:5).

14.9 – The Fast of Esther

All Jews have a custom, originating in the Gaonic period, to fast on the thirteenth of Adar in commemoration of the fasts that Esther observed before approaching King Achashveirosh to annul the decree (Esther 4:16) and the fast that the Jews observed on the thirteenth of Adar of that year. The wicked Haman decreed that all Jews – young and old, men, women, and children – be destroyed, killed, annihilated, and plundered on the thirteenth of Adar. Thanks to the Purim miracle, the hanging of Haman, and the rise of Mordechai and Esther, King Achashveirosh issued a second letter allowing the Jews to defend themselves and kill their enemies on that same day. The original decree, however, was not rescinded, because any decree written and signed by the king could not be annulled. Therefore, the enemies of Israel also had permission to kill the Jews. In other words, the kingdom established the thirteenth of Adar as the day on which the anti-Semites could destroy the Jews, but the Jews were permitted to fight back. And even though Mordechai was the king’s viceroy, the Jews were still in grave danger and in need of divine mercy, to help them overcome and kill their enemies. Therefore, the Jews who could not fight stirred themselves to repentance and fasted that day, as is Israel’s practice in times of trouble. And there is no greater penitence than that achieved by way of fasting, which purifies man’s material side and returns his spirituality to its natural, central place.

In commemoration of this fast, the Jewish people fast on the thirteenth of Adar every year. We still have enemies who want to destroy us and we still need to fast and repent every year anew.

In general, the laws of Ta’anit Esther (the Fast of Esther) are more lenient than those of the other minor fasts, because the other fasts were instituted by the Rabbis, while the Fast of Esther was established in consideration of Jewish custom. In practice, though, there is almost no difference between them (the note enumerates the differences).

The laws regarding the prayers and Torah-reading on Ta’anit Esther, for both Shacharit and Minchah, are the same as those of all the minor fasts. The only difference is that we omit Tachanun and Avinu Malkeinu from Minchah (according to Ashkenazim, who usually say Avinu Malkeinu [on fast days] in both Shacharit and Minchah), seeing that it is the day before Purim (M.B. 131:33). When the thirteenth of Adar coincides with Shabbat, we fast on the Thursday before, and since the fast is not on the eve of Purim, we pray Minchah as on all other fasts.

14.10 – In Commemoration of the Half-Shekel

People customarily give charity in the month of Adar in commemoration of the half-shekel that the Jews used to donate [to the Temple], in Adar, for the purpose of buying communal offerings. The best time to give this charity is immediately before Minchah on Ta’anit Esther, so that the charity can combine with the fast and achieve atonement (M.B. 694:4, K.H.C. 25).

Some have a custom to give a coin that equals half of the local currency [e.g., half a dollar, half a pound, etc.], while others give three such coins, corresponding to the three times it says terumah (donation) in Parashat Shekalim (Rema 694:1). The common coin in Israel today is the shekel, so, according to this custom, one should donate three half-shekel coins.

Some are accustomed to giving the equivalent of the [original] half-shekel, which is approximately ten grams of pure silver (K.H.C. 694:20). All of the customs are valid, and the more charity one gives the more blessing he receives.

Some hold that this custom applies only to men above the age of twenty, because they were obligated in this mitzvah [in Temple times] ( Rema). Others say that boys above the age of thirteen must uphold this custom, as well (Tosafot Yom Tov). A third opinion believes that one should give a donation in commemoration of the half-shekel for young children, too (Eliyah Rabbah, M.B. 694:5). Still others maintain that even women should give the half-shekel donation (K.H.C. 694:27). This is the most prevalent custom today, to donate at least one half-shekel for every member of the house, even an unborn fetus.

One should not use ma’aser kesafim money [one-tenth of one’s earnings set aside for charity] for this donation, for one is not allowed to fulfill an obligatory mitzvah or custom using ma’aser kesafim funds. However, one who has always performed the half-shekel commemoration according to the most stringent view and is now pressed for funds, making it difficult to uphold his custom without relying on ma’aser kesafim, may perform the mitzvah with his own money, according to the more lenient opinion – that is, a half-shekel per male above the age of twenty – and make up the rest with ma’aser kesafim money.


[1] According to the order of the Four Parshiot, at least one Shabbat in Adar is left without a parashah, and the Rabbis provided a mnemonic to help us remember which one to skip: ז-טו, ב-ו, ד-ד, ו-ביו. That is, if the first of Adar falls on the seventh (ז) day of the week (i.e. Shabbat), we do not read any of the [special] parashiot on the fifteenth (טו) of the month. If the first of Adar falls on a Monday (ב), no parashah is read on the Shabbat that coincides with the sixth (ו) of the month (when this happens, we read Shekalim at the end of the month of Shevat). If the first of Adar falls on a Wednesday (ד), we skip the Shabbat that coincides with the fourth (ד) of the month. And if the first of Adar falls on a Friday (ו), two Sabbaths are left without a parashah – the second (ב) and sixteenth (יו) of the month.

In the era of the Amora’im, the Jewish community in Babylonia had such a custom, but the Jews in Eretz Yisrael completed the Torah every three years. In contrast, the institution of the Four Parshiot and the special readings for the holidays is mentioned already in the Mishnah and the Gemara (Megillah 29a-30b).

Reading Parashat Zachor is a biblical commandment, as we will explain in halachah 6. Some say that Parashat Parah is also biblically ordained, as the Shulchan Aruch writes in O.C. 146:2, 685:7. Most authorities consider it a rabbinic enactment: see M.B. 146:13, 685:15; K.H.C. 28; Torat HaMo’adim 2:18. Ideally, one should treat Parashat Parah as strictly as he would Parashat Zachor.

If, however, he was born in the first Adar of a leap year and his bar mitzvah falls out on a leap year, as well, he marks the occasion in the first Adar.

Anniversary of Death (Yahrtzeit) : According to Sefardic custom, one who has a yahrtzeit in the month of Adar observes it, in a leap year, during the second Adar, for that is the main Adar in terms of Purim and the Four Parshiot. Ashkenazim, however, observe it during the first Adar, in order not to postpone the mitzvah. According to them, the second Adar is paramount only with regard to Purim and the Four Parshiot, not other matters. Moreover, since the second Adar is more joyous – after all, we celebrate Purim then – it is more appropriate to remember the dead in the first Adar. Some Ashkenazim act stringently and observe the yahrtzeit twice (Sh.A., Rama 568:7, M.B. 41). An Ashkenazi Jew who adopted a custom to fast in honor of a yahrtzeit on both Adars should continue his practice. Similarly, the Magen Avraham and Mishnah Berurah (42) write that one who took an explicit oath to fast on a yahrtzeit [which comes out in the month of Adar] must fast on both Adars [during a leap year]. But if a person died on a leap year, the family should observe the yahrtzeit, on subsequent leap years, in the same Adar on which he died.

Nonetheless, one fulfills his obligation [to hear Parashat Zachor] even by way of an inaccurate pronunciation, for as long as a large Jewish community accepts and agrees upon it, it is valid. Even regarding the mitzvah of Chalitza, where the man and woman must say certain verses in Hebrew, we do not insist that they enunciate them according to all the different traditions, and we permit the woman to get remarried (Iggrot Moshe, O.C. 3:5). In our community, we first read Parashat Zachor according to the accepted pronunciation used throughout the year. Then, only after the second blessing is finished, those who want to read it with different pronunciations come forth and do so. By allowing the variant readings only after the blessing, we show that everyone has already fulfilled their obligations with the regular one.

[7] One must remember Amalek verbally. After all, Chazal understand the prohibition against forgetting what Amalek did to us as a [passive] commandment, to remember in one’s heart (Megillah 18a). Therefore, when the Torah commands us to remember, it means through verbalization. The poskim dispute what exactly the biblical obligation entails. The Rambam and Ramban imply that reading from a Torah scroll is a rabbinic enactment, and that according to Torah law one can fulfill the mitzvah by reading from a Chumash or by any other means of remembering. (The Marcheshet [1:22] writes, based on the Ramban at the end of Ki Teitzei, that reading the Megillah on Purim also fulfills the mitzvah of remembering Amalek.) On the other hand, according to Tosafot, the Rashba (Berachot 13a), the Rosh, and the Eshkol, one is biblically obligated to hear Parashat Zachor from a Torah scroll. The Shulchan Aruch (O.C. 146:3) rules in accordance with this opinion. (See Mikra’ei Kodesh, Frank, Purim 5; Torat HaMo’adim 2:6.) Therefore, we bring a Torah scroll to a sick person or a prison inmate in order to read Parashat Zachor for him, which we do not do for any other reading ( M.B. 135:46; see also B.H. ibid.).

If the scroll from which Zachor was read is later found to be invalid, one need not reread the parashah from a different scroll ( Halichot Shlomo 18:4). After all, several poskim hold that one fulfills the mitzvah without a Torah scroll, and others assert that even though it is necessary to read Zachor from a Torah scroll, an invalid one is sufficient. It seems to me that if the mistake is found on that very Shabbat, the congregation should reread Parashat Zachor, since Chazal instituted the reading on that particular day (see Pri Megadim, Mishbatzot Zahav 143:1; Torat HaMo’adim p. 36).

The author of Terumat HaDeshen (108) derives from the words of the Rosh that, according to Torah Law, the mitzvah of Zachor must be performed by reading the parashah from a Torah scroll in the presence of ten [men]. Several Acharonim explain the reason as follows: remembering what the Amalekites did to us is supposed to arouse us to wage war against them, and since war is a communal obligation, Zachor must be read in public (Keren Orah, Maharam Schick). It seems to me that even the Terumat HaDeshen agrees that in the absence of a congregation there is [still] a mitzvah to read it individually. The Sha’ar HaTziyun (685:5) is puzzled by the Terumat HaDeshen’s viewpoint and believes that all the other Rishonim hold that a minyan is required only rabbinically (see Torat HaMo’adim 2:9). Practically speaking, if someone finds himself in a place where there is no minyan, he should read Zachor privately from a Torah scroll; and if no scroll is available, he should read it from a Chumash, for we already learned that some poskim hold that one fulfills the biblical obligation even without a Torah scroll (see Responsa Binyan Shlomo 54, K.H.C. 685:35).

According to the Magen Avraham, one who fails to hear Parashat Zachor on Shabbat can, be’di’avad (ex post facto), discharge his obligation by hearing the Torah reading on Purim, [starting with the words] Amalek came [and fought with Israel ( Shemot 17:8-16)]. The Mishnah Berurah (685:16) questions this, because [that section] only recounts the narrative of the war against Amalek, not the commandment to remember and eradicate him. Some authorities suggest that [he should wait until later in the year] when the congregation reads Parashat Ki Teitzei, at the end of which Parashat Zachor is written, and ask the reader to have in mind to absolve him of his obligation to remember Amalek. Both the reader and listener must have intention to fulfill the mitzvah ( Mikra’ei Kodesh, Frank, Purim 6). In my humble opinion, however, it is best for him to read Parashat Zachor privately, from a Torah scroll, as soon as possible, in case he might forget it altogether. This way, he will fulfill his obligation according to the vast majority of poskim. Then, if he remembers, he can act scrupulously and try to fulfill the mitzvah through the readings of Purim and Ki Teitzei.

The Acharonim dispute the reason for this mitzvah. According to Keren Orah, it is an impetus to destroy Amalek. Therefore, once he is wiped out, the mitzvah ceases to exist. The author of Melechet Shlomo holds that the mitzvah to remember Amalek will remain in effect even after that nation is destroyed, because of the fundamentals of belief that it contains. (See the Talmudic Encyclopedia under Zechirat Ma’aseh Amalek, notes 33-34.)

[8] The Rishonim dispute whether or not mitzvot require intent. The Shulchan Aruch (O.C. 60:4) rules that they do require intent. After the fact (be’di’avad), one discharges his obligation even if he did not have explicit intent, as long as he knows that the reading of Zachor is designed to fulfill the mitzvah of remembering Amalek, for that is [considered] latent intent. One who has no idea why we read Zachor, however, does not discharge his obligation [by simply hearing the parashah], according to those who require intent (Chayei Adam, M.B. 60:10). One who fails to hear all the words fulfills the mitzvah nonetheless, as long as he hears the main point – to remember what Amalek did to us (Halichot Shlomo 18:1).

[9] Zachor is not considered a time-bound mitzvah because one can fulfill it by remembering Amalek once, and according to the Torah there is no set time for its performance. However, many poskim hold that women are exempt because they do not go out to war (Sefer HaChinuch 603). One opinion holds that the Torah does not require a yearly reading of Zachor; rather, one can fulfill the commandment biblically through any form of remembrance, once every few years. The author of Torat Chesed (37) writes that according to this opinion, Parashat Zachor is only a rabbinic mitzvah, incumbent on men alone. The Avnei Neizer (O.C. 509) and Marcheshet (1:22) also hold that women are exempt. Some authorities maintain that women are obligated [in this mitzvah], because even women must take part in a milchemet mitzvah – religious war (Minchat Chinuch). The Ridvaz (on the Rambam, Hilchot Melachim 7:4) explains that they are required to supply [the soldiers] with food and water. Responsa Binyan Tzion (2:8) adds that something positive will [surely] result from women remembering Amalek, just like Yael killed Sisra, Esther put Haman to death, and Yehudit killed the Greek officer. See Halichot Beitah 9:5-6, with note 8; Yabia Omer 8:54.

Therefore, the best practice is for women to hear Parashat Zachor in a minyan. If that is impossible, someone should read it for them from a scroll, without a minyan. If that, too, is unfeasible, they should read it to themselves from a Chumash. Actually, some Acharonim write that we do not take out a Torah scroll for the sake of women. This can be found in Responsa Kinyan Torah 7:53 and Halichot Beitah 9:8. However, I do not understand what the problem is; according to the letter of the law, one may take out a Torah scroll in order to learn from it. Many communities, therefore, have a custom to take out a Torah and read Zachor just for women, as is cited in Minchat Yitzchak 9:68 and Torat HaMo’adim 2:13.

[10] The Rambam holds that it is permissible to accept converts from Amalek. According to him, the halachah does not follow R. Eliezer’s opinion in the Mechilta, end of BeShalach, because another opinion is cited there, that of R. Elazar HaModa’i. In addition, the Gemara about Haman’s descendants goes against R. Eliezer’s viewpoint. The Chida (Ya’ir Ozen, Gimmel,1) writes in a similar vein. The Chazon Ish (Y.D. 157:5) explains that R. Eliezer prohibits the conversion of an Amalekite only during war time. According to the Avnei Neizer (O.C. 508), we may grant an Amalekite the status of ger toshav [lit., “resident proselyte” – a non-Jew who adopts the seven Noachide laws and is thus allowed to live in our Land], who is subservient to the Jews, but he may not become a regular convert.

Others prohibit the conversion of Amalekites, interpreting the statement that Haman’s descendants taught Torah in B’nei Brak in various ways: Some say that we determine the status of a non-Jew based on his father’s nationality, and these teachers descended from Haman via a daughter (Mahari Engel, Gilyonei HaShas, Gittin 57b; Maharsham 3:272). Others explain that we do not accept them at first, but if they are accepted by accident, their conversion is valid (Yeshu’ot Malko. Likutim 15). Another authority claims that the conversion is valid only if the mistake is discovered several generations later, but if one’s Amalekite origin is revealed immediately, the conversion is null and void (Shevut Yehudah on the Mechilta). Another explanation states that the teachers descended from an Amalekite who assimilated into another nation and converted afterwards. In such a case, it is impossible to know that he is from Amalek, and we accept him. Later on, it was revealed, through Ruach HaKodesh (Divine Inspiration), that these teachers were descendants of Haman, and their conversion was valid (R. Chayim Palagi, Einei Kol Chai, Sanhedrin 96b). It is possible to say that an Amalekite can divest himself of the status of Amalek by accepting the seven Noachide laws, and then, at a later stage, convert (suggested by the Chida, Petach Einayim, Sanhedrin 96b). According to the author of Kol Mevaser (2:42), it is permissible to convert an Amalekite; the Mechilta only prohibits [such a convert] from marrying into the Jewish people. See also Tzitz Eliezer 13:71; Chazon Ovadyah on Purim, pp. 11-15.

[11] The Rishonim ask, how could the Rabbis establish a fast day on the eve of Purim? Megillat Ta’anit states explicitly that we do not fast or deliver eulogies on the day before a Rabbinic holiday. The authors of Tosafot answer that since Megillat Ta’anit was repealed, the law regarding the day before [the holidays mentioned therein] was also repealed. Rabbeinu Zerachyah HaLevi explains that Purim has the status of a Torah-based holiday, which needs no reinforcement, and one may fast before it. The Rosh and the Ran point out that [this fast] is alluded to in Megillat Esther (9:31) – The matter of the fasts and their outcry – therefore, one may fast prior to the holiday. The Beit Yosef elaborates on this in section 686.

[12] Since the Fast of Esther is based on custom, we are more lenient in cases of doubt. Thus, a woman is exempt from fasting for twenty-four months after giving birth. Even though the poskim dispute this law when it comes to the other minor fasts – the majority taking a more stringent approach, as explained above 7.8 – there is room to rule leniently regarding the Fast of Esther.

A bride and groom during the seven-day period of joy after their wedding, as well as the “masters” of a brit milah (the father,mohel, and sandek), must observe the other minor fasts but are exempt from fasting on Ta’anit Esther. This is stated in Sha’ar HaTziyun 686:16, Kaf HaChayim 686:16, 28, and Yechaveh Da’at 2:78. (According to the Gra, they are exempt from all the minor fasts.) The Rama, 686:2, maintains that the “masters” of a brit must fast [on Ta’anit Esther], and many conduct themselves in this manner. With regard to a bride and groom, however, the custom is to be lenient; see Mikra’ei Kodesh, by Harari, 3:7, and Piskei Teshuvot 686:4.

The Rama (686:2) writes that those who have very painful eye-aches need not fast [on Ta’anit Esther], but they should make up the fast a different day. Most Acharonim explain his intention as follows: even though they are not considered [truly] sick, they may postpone their fast to a different day, since they are in pain. This is not the case regarding the other minor fasts, on which people must fast even if they are in pain. In practice, it is difficult to distinguish between one who is sick and one who is [merely] in pain. Likewise, R. Harari writes in his Mikra’ei Kodesh (chap. 3, n. 25), quoting R. Shaul Yisraeli and R. Mordechai Eliyahu, that those who suffer from eye-aches need not fast [on Ta’anit Esther], just like a sick person, and they need not compensate for the missed fast. On the minor fasts, it is proper to act stringently and refrain from washing oneself and playing music, as a sign of mourning over the Temple’s destruction (see above 7.2, n. 3). On the Fast of Esther, however, there is no reason to adopt this stringency.
