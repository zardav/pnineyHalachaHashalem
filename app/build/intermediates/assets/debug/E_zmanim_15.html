Chapter Fifteen: Purim and the Reading of the Megillah

15.1 – The Miracle of Purim

The joy of Purim demonstrates Israel’s eternal holiness. Even though this holiness is sometimes hidden, due to our sins, it never disappears. Even when the Jewish people sin, they are still called God’s children, and He directs the world and arranges events for the benefit of His people, in order to save and redeem them.

At the time [of the Purim story], the Jews were in dire straits. The First Temple was destroyed and Israel was exiled from its Land. True, Cyrus had already declared that the Jews could return home, but only a small minority [heeded his call]. The Persian Empire ruled the world, and the large Jewish population living throughout the Empire tried to assimilate and act as the Gentiles do, to the point where many were willing to bow down to an idol. In the capital city of Shushan, [many] Jews partook in Achashveirosh’s feast and looked on as [the Persians] took out the Temple vessels, which our enemies had pillaged at the time of the Churban, and used them for mundane purposes. They nevertheless took pleasure from this wicked man’s feast. It seemed as though the great vision for which the Jewish nation was chosen was steadily vanishing; there would no longer be hope for a return to Zion; the Jewish people would no longer bring the word of God to the world.

Then, a great accusation arose in heaven against Israel. Despite the fact that HaShem chose the Jews from among all the other nations, gave them His Torah, and planted His Shechinah (Divine Presence) in their midst, they act like the Gentiles, bow to an idol, and refuse to ascend to their Land in order to build the Holy Temple! Therefore, the wicked Haman, a descendant of Amalek, rose up and drove the Persian Empire to enact a frightful decree [against the Jews], the likes of which had never been seen before: To destroy, to kill, and to annihilate all the Jews, from young to old, children and women, in one day, on the thirteenth [day] of the twelfth month, which is the month of Adar, and to plunder their spoils (Esther 3:13).

By the way, there were Jews who contended with Mordechai, claiming that he was the cause of the evil decree: by refusing to bow to the wicked Haman, he stirred his wrath against all the Jews (ibid. 3:2-6).

In the end, it became clear that the Holy One, blessed be He, is the One Who arranges everything; He had already prepared the cure for the ailment, by having Achashveirosh take Esther [as his wife]. This way, Mordechai and Esther were able to thwart Haman’s plan, and turn everything around. Instead of Israel’s enemies carrying out their [evil] scheme, the Jews killed their enemies, even hanging Haman and his sons on the tree that he had prepared for Mordechai. The Jews experienced a great salvation; their pride flourished among the nations; and they mustered the strength to ascend to Eretz Yisrael and settle it, eventually rebuilding the Holy Temple.

15.2 – Accepting the Torah Anew

If we delve deeper, we will see that Haman’s decree [actually] awakened Israel’s unique inner quality (segulah). The decree made it clear that the Jews were willing to make great sacrifices in order to hold onto their faith. After all, they could have assimilated into the gentile culture and save themselves from annihilation. Nevertheless, they did not try to escape their Jewish destiny. On the contrary, the decree stirred them to repent and strengthen their faith and commitment to Torah and mitzvot.

So great was that moment that our Sages state that Israel re-accepted the Torah at the time of Achashveirosh. In a certain sense, their renewed commitment at that time was greater than their original acceptance of the Torah on Mt. Sinai. When the Torah was first given, the Jews accepted it forcibly, as it says, They stood at the bottom of the mountain (Shemot 19:17). Chazal comment:

This teaches that the Holy One, blessed be He, held the mountain over them like a pail, and said, “If you accept the Torah – good; and if not – here will be your burial place.” R. Acha son of Ya’akov said, “This is a great declaration regarding the Torah” (i.e., a declaration of annulment, for since they accepted the Torah under duress, they are not obligated to keep it). Rava said, “Even so, they re-accepted it at the time of Achashveirosh, as it says, The Jews confirmed and accepted (Esther 9:27) – [that is], they confirmed what they had previously accepted.” (Shabbat 88a)

Many [commentators] explain that [HaShem] “held the mountain [over them] like a pail” in a spiritual sense. After all the great miracles of the exodus from Egypt, the splitting of the sea, and the awesome revelations at Mt. Sinai, it was impossible for the Jews not to accept the Torah. However, the question still remained: will the Jews stay connected to HaShem and His Torah even afterwards, when they are detached from the miracles and wonders? Indeed, there were ups and downs, until the days of Purim arrived. That is when it became clear that Am Yisrael’s connection to its faith and Torah were absolute. The fearful decree made it clear that the price of belief might be too heavy to bear. Nevertheless, the Jews chose to adhere to their faith, repent, and pray to God, without any coercion. Not only did they return to observe the 613 mitzvot, they even instituted additional mitzvot after they were saved – the mitzvot of Purim.

This way, we were privileged to build the Second Temple, and a door was opened for the advancement of the learning of the Oral Law, which was the main spiritual enterprise of the Second Temple era.

15.3 – Establishing Purim as an Everlasting Holiday

Even though the joy over the salvation was great, it was initially unclear how we were to mark the event. Esther sent [a request] to the Sages, “Write me down for posterity,” meaning, write down the Purim story and include it in the Holy Scriptures (Tanach). Esther further requested, “Establish me for posterity,” meaning, establish the days of Purim as days of joy and reading the Megillah for future generations. At first, the Rabbis had doubts about this, both because it might arouse [feelings of] vengeance among the gentile nations when they see Israel rejoicing over their downfall, and also because they were unsure whether it was appropriate to add to the Bible another description of Israel’s war against Amalek. In the end, they found scriptural allusions that indicate that there is room to write about the battle of Amalek once again. So, the Men of the Great Assembly composed Megillat Esther (the Scroll of Esther) with Ruach HaKodesh (Divine Inspiration) and established Purim as an eternal holiday (Megillah 2a, 7a; Bava Batra 15a).

The Men of the Great Assembly (Anshei Knesset HaGedolah) constituted the

Supreme Rabbinic Court
that functioned at the beginning of the Second Commonwealth, and it consisted of 120 elders, including prophets and sages. Among them were Chaggai, Zecharyah, Malachi, Daniel, Chananyah, Mishael, Azaryah, Ezra the Scribe, Nechemyah son of Chachalyah, Mordechai the Jew [of the Megillah], and Zerubavel son of She’altiel. Ezra the Scribe was the most prominent of all, so much so that the Anshei Knesset HaGedolah is sometimes called Ezra’s Beit Din. They comprised the
Supreme Rabbinic Court
that established the first major enactments that are considered Rabbinic mitzvot, and they were the impetus for the continued activity of the Sages of the Oral Law.

The Purim miracle is considered the last miracle that was allowed to be recorded in the Tanach, as Chazal state, “Esther is the end of all the miracles.” Thus, in effect, the writing of Megillat Esther sealed the Tanach.

Purim is the link between the Written and Oral Laws. This is reflected in the status of its mitzvot, which are mi’divrei kabbalah, a category between Torah commandments and Rabbinic ones. On the one hand, they are not as exalted as mitzvot that are written in the Pentateuch. On the other hand, they are not considered Rabbinic mitzvot, seeing that Megillat Esther is included in Scriptures. The Rishonim dispute how one should act if a doubt arises regarding the laws of Purim: does the halachah demand that one act strictly, as in cases of Torah precepts, or leniently, as in cases of Rabbinic mitzvot.

One must perform seven mitzvot on Purim. Four of them are unique to Purim: 1) reading the Megillah, 2) sending portions of food to one another, 3) giving gifts to the poor, and 4) participating in a festive meal. The remaining three are Rabbinic precepts that pertain to other holidays, as well: 1) reading the Torah (for men), 2) mentioning [the uniqueness of] the day by reciting Al HaNissim in our prayers and in Birkat HaMazone (Grace After Meals), and 3) refraining from fasting and delivering eulogies.

15.4 – Walled and Unwalled Cities

There is a very special halachah pertaining to Purim: it has two dates. Most places celebrate on the fourteenth of Adar, while cities that were surrounded by a wall at the time of Yehoshua bin Nun, as well as Achashveirosh’s capital city Shushan, celebrate on the fifteenth.

In order to understand the reason for this distinction, we must first remember the order of events. Haman’s decree stated that on the thirteenth of Adar all the enemies of Israel could destroy, kill, and annihilate all the Jews in the world. And the decree was not abolished even after Mordechai and Esther’s wondrous rise [to power], because the law stated that any decree written [in the king’s name] and sealed with the king’s signet ring could not be revoked. Therefore, the only thing they could do was to issue an additional edict, sealed with the king’s ring, allowing the Jews to defend themselves and kill their enemies.

Until the thirteenth of Adar, it was unclear how matters would develop. Granted, the Jews were already given permission to defend themselves without interference from the Persian army, but who knew if they would succeed in defeating their enemies? Then, on the thirteenth of Adar, a fear of the Jews descended upon the nations, and the Jews were able to defeat their enemies. The next day, the fourteenth of Adar, the Jews rested from battle and made it into a day of feasting and joy. In Shushan, the capital, however, the haters of Israel were numerous, and the Jews were unable to kill them all in one day. Therefore, Queen Esther came before King Achashveirosh and asked him to grant the Jews permission to take revenge against their enemies for one more day, which he did. So the Jews of Shushan continued eradicating their enemies on the fourteenth of Adar and rested on the fifteenth, making it into a day of feasting and joy.

Since Purim was celebrated on two separate days from the very beginning, [the Rabbis] instituted that we bring this to light throughout the generations. Therefore, most places celebrate Purim on the fourteenth, while Shushan, where the miracle was greater – because all the events of the Megillah occurred there and the Jews took revenge against their enemies there for two days – celebrates Purim on the fifteenth. [Our Sages] also enacted that all prominent cities, like Shushan, celebrate on the fifteenth [as well]; and the indicator of a city’s importance is having a surrounding wall, like Shushan did.

At that time, however, Eretz Yisrael was in ruins, and had they based their enactment on that time period, not one city in all of Eretz Yisrael would celebrate Purim on the fifteenth of Adar, because none of them had a wall. Therefore, in honor of the Holy Land, they decided that all cities surrounded by a wall at the time of Yehoshua bin Nun would celebrate Purim on the fifteenth, even if they were currently destroyed, while all other cities would celebrate on the fourteenth. The only exception is Shushan, the capital, for even though it was established after Yehoshua’s time, it celebrates on the fifteenth, since the miracle occurred there.

Today, Jerusalem is the only city that celebrates Purim on the fifteenth of Adar, because it is the only place about which we have a clear tradition that it was surrounded by a wall at the time of Yehoshua bin Nun. Regarding some cities it is unclear [whether or not they had a wall at that time]. There is even doubt about Shushan’s [exact] location. We will elaborate on these laws in chapter seventeen.

15.5 – Reading the Megillah and Publicizing the Miracle

Everyone is obligated in [the mitzvah of] reading the Megillah – men, women, and converts. One who hears someone else’s reading discharges his obligation, as long as he hears it from one who is obligated in the mitzvah. However, one who hears it from a minor, who is not required to keep mitzvot, has not fulfilled his obligation (Sh.A. 689:1-2).

The main purpose of reading the Megillah is to publicize the miracle and demonstrate that HaShem runs and oversees the world, directing everything for the best. Even the worst troubles eventually turn around for the good. This [understanding] strengthens people’s faith [in God] and stimulates them to do more to reveal His name and rectify the world.

Reading the Megillah before a multitude of people in order to publicize the miracle is so important that even the kohanim who worked in the Beit HaMikdash would delay the offering of the daily Tamid sacrifice in order to hear the Megillah reading in a congregation. Only afterwards would they offer the Tamid. Similarly, Torah scholars who are involved in the study of Torah, and are able to hear the Megillah with a minyan near their study hall, should nevertheless interrupt their learning in order to go to a synagogue and hear the Megillah together with the masses (Megillah 3a).

Therefore, the members of a synagogue that usually hosts several minyanim a day must try to gather together on Purim and hear the Megillah with a large number of people. However, one who is accustomed to praying in a small synagogue need not change his fixed practice in order to hear the Megillah in a large synagogue, provided that there will be ten men at his reading (Sh.A. 687:2, Chayei Adam, M.B. 7, Sha’ar HaTziyun 8, 10).

Only as a last resort – if one is unable to hear it in a minyan – may one fulfill the mitzvah in private, with a blessing (Sh.A. and Rama 690:18).

15.6 – When to Read the Megillah

One must read the Megillah at night and once again during the day, to commemorate the fact that the Jews cried out to God in their time of need during the day and at night (Megillah 4a; Rashi ibid.).

The Megillah reading at night may be done all night – from the emergence of the stars to daybreak, while the daytime reading may be accomplished all day long – from sunrise (and be’di’avad, from daybreak) until sunset. [However], the zealous fulfill mitzvot promptly, reading the Megillah at night immediately after Ma’ariv and during the day immediately after Shacharit (Sh.A. 687:1, 693:1, 693:4).

It is forbidden to eat or sleep before reading the Megillah at night. Learning Torah, however, is permissible. One who finds it difficult to extend the Fast of Esther until after the Megillah reading may drink beforehand, on condition that he avoids intoxicating drinks. Similarly, one who is very hungry may eat a casual meal before Megillah reading. That is, [he may eat] unlimited amounts of fruit and up to an egg-size of foods that require a mezonot blessing (Sh.A. 232:3, M.B. 35, Rama 692:4, M.A. 7, M.B. 14-15).

The same laws apply to the daytime reading. However, since it takes place immediately after Shacharit, one must [also] be careful about all the prohibitions that apply prior to Shacharit, which are more stringent. Nonetheless, one who already prayed Shacharit and has yet to hear the Megillah should not eat before fulfilling the mitzvah. Under pressing circumstances, however, one may eat a casual meal before hearing the Megillah. A woman, as well, may not eat before hearing the Megillah, and if she is very hungry, she may eat a snack, but not a set/full meal (M.B. 692:15-16. In time of very great need, she may ask someone to remind her [to hear the Megillah] and then she may eat a full meal before the reading).

Some of the greatest Rishonim maintain that the primary [fulfillment of the] mitzvah of reading the Megillah and publicizing the miracle occurs during the day, like all the other mitzvot of Purim. Therefore, one must be more meticulous about the daytime reading and try even harder to read it in front of a multitude of people, or at least in a minyan.

15.7 – Women and Megillah Reading

According to Rashi and the Rambam, women are obligated to read or hear the Megillah the same way men are, and a woman may [therefore] read the Megillah for her family. In contrast, the Behag and Rabbeinu Chananel hold that a woman’s obligation is different from that of a man. Men are obligated in reading the Megillah, while women are only obligated in hearing it. According to this opinion, a woman cannot absolve her husband of his obligation. The author of Responsa Avnei Neizer (O.C. 511) explains that the difference stems from the fact that women need to hear the Megillah solely in order to publicize the miracle; therefore, they are obligated only to hear it, not read it. Men, on the other hand, are commanded not only to publicize the miracle but also to remember Amalek in order to inspire themselves to eradicate [this evil nation]. Therefore, men are also obligated to read the Megillah.

Since this halachah is under dispute, with an equal number of Rishonim on each side, most Acharonim have determined that a woman should not absolve a man of his obligation to read/hear the Megillah. Only under extenuating circumstances, when a man cannot read for himself or hear it from another man, may a woman read the Megillah for him. This way he will [at least] fulfill the mitzvah according to those who hold that a woman can read on behalf of a man.

A woman may absolve other women of their obligation. However, some say that a woman may not read on behalf of many women, because the law of reading the Megillah for the many is like that of reading the Torah, and just as women do not read the Torah, they should also not read the Megillah for many women. Some say that when the Megillah is read for women no blessing is recited (Ben Ish Chai, Shannah Alef, TeTzaveh 1; K.H.C. 689:19). However, the halachah follows the vast majority of poskim who maintain that a woman may read the Megillah on behalf of other women, reciting the same blessing a man recites. And if ten women are present, she also recites the blessing of HaRav et Riveinu after the reading. Ideally, though, it is preferable for women to hear the Megillah from a man, in order to fulfill their obligation according to all opinions. Even better is for a woman to hear the Megillah in the synagogue along with the men, for the more people there are the more the miracle is publicized.

When a man reads the Megillah for women, the prevalent custom is for the reader to recite the blessing for everyone; and if ten are present, he recites the blessing of HaRav et Riveinu after the reading. Others follow a custom in which one woman recites the blessing for the rest. Both customs are valid.

15.8 – Young Children

It is a mitzvah to teach children how to perform mitzvot, and from the moment a boy or girl reaches the stage at which he or she understands the Megillah and can listen to it as the halachah requires, one must teach them to do so. This matter does not depend on a child’s age, but on his or her personal development. And since the Megillah reading is quite long, most children reach the stage at which they can listen to the entire Megillah properly after the age of nine.

It is a good practice to bring younger children – five or six years of age – to the synagogue to hear the Megillah. Even though they are not yet old enough to be educated in [the mitzvah of] Megillah reading – seeing that they cannot be careful [enough] to hear the entire thing – it is still good to bring them, because they understand the main gist. However, one should not bring small children to the synagogue, if they are liable to interfere [and make it difficult for others] to hear the Megillah. One must be careful not to be meticulous about his children’s education at the expense of the other synagogue attendees, who might suffer because of his children’s disturbances.

In order to stimulate joy and grab the children’s attention, there is a custom for the congregation to read four verses aloud, verses that signal the essential beginning and end of the miracle. Afterwards, the cantor rereads them from the scroll (Rama 690:17; M.B. 689:16; based on the Mordechai and the Levush). (The verses are: A Jewish man [2:5], And Mordechai went out [8:15], For the Jews [8:16], and For Mordechai [10:3]).

It seems that the purpose of the custom to “smite” Haman with noisemakers is, likewise, to excite the children during the Megillah reading. However, one must be careful not to turn the trivial into the essential and make noise when the cantor continues to read the Megillah (Sh.A. 690:17; also see M.B. 59 and B.H.).

15.9 – The Megillah

The Scroll of Esther is considered one of the Holy Writings; therefore, it must be written in the way a Torah scroll is written, with black ink on parchment. If it is written using something other than ink, or on paper, it is invalid, and one who reads from it has not fulfilled his obligation. The hide [from which the parchment is made] must be processed for the sake of writing a Megillah. The [individual] sheets [of parchment] must be sown together with [threads made of] sinews. One must etch out the lines using an engraving tool before [commencing] the writing, so that the words come out straight. And it must be written by hand, having in mind the sanctity of the Megillah.

When writing a Torah scroll, we make sure that all of its letters are written according to their exact configurations, that no letters touch each other, and – obviously – that no letters are left out or added. Similarly, [a scribe] should be careful [about all these things] when writing a Megillah. Be’di’avad, however, there is a difference between a Torah scroll and Megillat Esther. The halachah states that one may not recite the blessing over the reading of the Torah if the scroll contains a mistake, even in just one letter. When it comes to the Megillah, however, the halachah allows one to read from a scroll that is missing a portion of its letters, if a perfectly kosher one is unavailable. For example, if a scribe mistakenly omitted many letters from a Megillah, or wrote them incorrectly – or if he originally wrote the scroll properly, but some of its letters faded over time – one may [still] use it to perform the mitzvah, as long as the essentials are written [properly]. The reason being, that the Megillah is called a “letter,” showing that its purpose is to tell the story from a written document, but it need not be as precise as a [Torah] scroll needs to be. We learn from here that one fulfills his obligation as long as the essentials of the Megillah are written properly, and on condition that one fills in the missing words, by reading them from a printed Megillah or saying them by heart.

According to the letter of the law, it is permissible to write a translation of the Megillah, with ink on parchment, for someone who does not understand Hebrew, and by reading this translation, he will fulfill his obligation to read the Megillah. For example, someone who knows only English may write an English translation of the Megillah, in ink on parchment, and read from it… (Sh.A. 690:8-11). This is not carried out in practice, however, for we do not know how to translate the words precisely. Rather, [one who speaks a foreign language] fulfills his obligation by hearing the Megillah in Hebrew, for even though he does not understand the words, he has intention to fulfill the mitzvah of Megillah reading (Sh.A. 690:8, M.B. 32, A.H.Sh. 15).

15.10 – The Mitzvah of Reading and the Law of One Who Missed a Word

The mitzvah of reading the Megillah [is accomplished] by reading it from a kosher Megillah, written in ink on parchment. If one recites it by heart or reads it from a printed book, he has not discharged his obligation (Sh.A. 690:3). One who reads the majority from a [kosher] Megillah and the rest from a book or from memory has fulfilled his obligation, as long as he says the entire Megillah without missing a word (as we explained in the previous halachah).

However, if the reader skips a word, or makes a mistake in one of the words – assuming that the mistake changes the meaning of the word – he has failed to discharge his obligation according to most poskim, and he must reread the Megillah properly.

Even when the reader reads all the words properly, one of the listeners will not discharge his obligation if he fails to hear one of the words. In practice, this is the most important halachah regarding Megillah reading, because people sometimes fail to hear all of the words, due to the excess noise caused by the presence of children in the synagogue. This problem becomes especially acute after Haman’s name is read, for the reader sometimes continues reading before the children finish making noise, and those who sit in the back miss a word, causing them to forfeit the entire reading.

The solution is to immediately read the missed word or words from the printed Megillah one is using. And if, in the meantime, the reader continues to read ahead, [the listener who missed a word] should continue reading until he catches up with the reader. Even though the printed book in front of him is not a kosher Megillah, he may – be’di’avad – use it to fill in the missing words, seeing that has heard most of the Megillah from a kosher scroll. However, when listening to the [rest of] the Megillah from the reader, one should not read along with him from a printed version.

15.11 – The Laws of Reading the Megillah

[When not in use], it is proper to leave a Megillah rolled up from end to beginning. However, since [Megillat Esther] is referred to as a “letter,” the custom is to spread it out and fold it over, leaf over leaf, before reading it in public, in order to publicize the miracle. When the reading is completed, the Megillah is rolled up again from end to beginning, out of respect, so that the Megillah not remain opened. Only after the Megillah is rolled up does the cantor recite the blessing of HaRav et Riveinu (Sh.A. 690:17, M.B. 55-56, K.H.C. 102-105).

One may fulfill the mitzvah of reading the Megillah either sitting or standing. The only one who needs to stand is the reader, out of respect for the congregation (Sh.A. 690:1). Most Jews have a custom to stand for the blessings (M.B. 690:1; Ben Ish Chai, TeTzaveh 4; see also K.H.C. 2).

The Megillah should be read with its cantillation notes, but if no one knows how to do so, it may be read without the notes, be’di’avad ( Sha’arei Teshuvah 690:1).

One must read the Megillah in order, for if one reads it backwards – that is, in an order other than the way it is written – he does not fulfill his obligation. For example, if one misses a word or a verse during the reading, he should not say, “I’ll continue listening to the Megillah until the end, and I’ll make up what I missed afterwards.” Rather, he must immediately fill in the missing word(s), catch up to the reader, and continue listening to the reading, in order, until the end of the Megillah.

One who dozes off during the Megillah reading does not discharged his obligation, for he certainly fails to hear some words (Sh.A. 690:12). As we learned above (halachah 9), one who hears the Megillah fulfills his obligation even if he does not understand Hebrew.

Ideally (le’chatchillah), one should read the Megillah successively, but be’di’avad, if one interrupted [the reading in the middle] – remaining silent or even speaking – he has not forfeited what he already read and may continue reading from where he left off (Sh.A. 690:5; also see M.B. 18 and Sh.A., O.C. 65).

Many authorities hold that one who hears the Megillah through an electronic device, like a telephone, radio, or loudspeaker, does not fulfill his obligation. After all, such devices receive a person’s voice as electronic signals and transform it back into a new voice. Therefore, it is like hearing a recording [of the Megillah reading], which is invalid. Ideally, one should heed this opinion.

15.12 – The Blessings and the Order of the Reading

We recite three blessings before the nighttime reading: “Blessed are you, O Lord, our God, King of the Universe, Who has sanctified us with His commandments and commanded us regarding the reading of the Megillah,” “Blessed are you, O Lord, our God, King of the Universe, Who has performed miracles for our forefathers, in those days, at this time,” “Blessed are you, O Lord, our God, King of the Universe, Who has kept us alive, sustained us, and brought us to this time.”

The blessings prepare us for the mitzvah, for they stimulate the reader and the listeners to focus their thoughts on the fulfillment of the mitzvah and to understand its purpose, which is to remember and publicize the miracle that HaShem did for our ancestors. Nevertheless, one who reads without reciting a blessing fulfills his obligation, as long as he intends to fulfill the mitzvah (Sh.A. 690:14).

There are varying customs regarding the recital of the Shehechiyanu blessing during the day. The Sefardim do not say it because the Shehechiyanu recited at night covers the entire day. The Ashkenazim repeat the blessing prior to the daytime reading, because that reading is a separate mitzvah. Moreover, it is the primary reading; therefore, it cannot be “dragged along” by the nighttime reading (Sh.A. 692:1).

When saying Shehechiyanu before the Megillah reading, it is proper to have in mind [that the blessing also applies to] the other mitzvot of the day: Mishlo’ach Manot (sending gifts of food to one another), Matanot LaEvyonim (giving charity), and the [festive] meal. Sefardim do this at night, while Ashkenazim do so during the day (M.B. 692:1).

The [prevalent] custom is to recite HaRav et Riveinu – a blessing of praise and thanksgiving – when the Megillah reading is over and the scroll is rolled back to the beginning. Most poskim agree that this blessing is recited only when the Megillah is read before ten men or women…

15.13 – Taking Revenge on Haman and his Ten Sons

The killing of Haman and his ten sons is an integral part of the Megillah, for [it confirms] that justice was done and the wicked ones who rose up against the Jewish people were punished and killed. Anyone who rises up against Israel, God’s nation, [in effect] rises up against God, creator and sustainer of the world, and – according to strict justice – deserves total annihilation. Several laws demonstrate the significance of their deaths.

Firstly, we write out the [names] of Haman’s ten sons in the format of a song, but not like all other songs [in Scriptures]. In all other songs, like the Song at the Sea, the words and spaces are interwoven. The killing of Haman’s sons, in contrast, is written in a straight and organized fashion. One word is written on the two ends of each line and a space is left in the middle. Thus, the names of the ten sons are written on the right side and the [Hebrew] word et, which connects them, is written on the left side (Megillah 16b, Sh.A. 691:3). The explanation is [as follows]: The purpose of all [other] songs is to convey [the extent of] the salvation that Israel experienced; therefore, they are “spacious” and expansive. The song describing the killing of Haman’s sons, however, expresses the joy we feel over the fact that they were utterly destroyed and that exact justice was meted out; therefore, it is written in a sealed, linear style (Maharal, Or Chadash 9:10).

One must try to read all the names in a single breath, to demonstrate that they all died simultaneously. If one fails to do so, he has nonetheless fulfilled his obligation, be’di’avad. The letter vav in the name Vaizata is written higher than the other letters, to teach that [Haman’s sons] were all hanged together (Megillah 16b; Sh.A. 690:15, 691:4). The point is this: The foundation of Israel’s belief is the existence of one God. The Amalakites oppose this belief and hate the Jews. So, when they are annihilated, God’s oneness is revealed to the world. Therefore, when [Haman’s sons] were punished, they died as one, for their deaths verified the belief in God’s unity (Maharal, loc. cit.).

After the Megillah reading, one must say, “Cursed be Haman, blessed be Mordechai; cursed be Zeresh, blessed be Esther; cursed be all the wicked ones, blessed be all the righteous ones; and may Charvonah be remembered for good (Yerushalmi, Megillah 3:7; Sh.A. 690:16).

In the period of the Rishonim, a custom began to spread among the children, and the adults as well, to bang when Haman’s name is read. Apparently, they wanted to express their hatred for the wicked and their joy over their downfall. Even though there is no source for this practice, the Rama writes, “One should not cancel or deride any custom, for it was not established for naught” (690:17). Indeed, some Rishonim disregard the custom, and some Acharonim even come out against it, because the noise is liable to prevent the listeners from fulfilling their obligation to read/hear the Megillah (as explained above in sec. 10). In practice, people may continue following the custom of “hitting” Haman, as long as they make sure that everyone can hear the entire Megillah properly.

15.14 – Al HaNissim, Torah Reading, Eulogies, and Tachanun

The Rabbis formulated the Al HaNissim prayer so that we may thank HaShem for the salvation He performed for the Jewish people at the time of Purim. We recite it in the blessing of thanksgiving in Shemoneh Esrei and in the blessing of Nodeh Lecha (“We thank You”) in Birkat HaMazone (Grace After Meals). We do not mention Purim in the abbreviated Grace After meals [known as Al HaMichyah].

If one forgets to say Al HaNissim in Shemoneh Esrei or Birkat HaMazone, he [nonetheless] fulfills his obligation. If, however, he remembers before concluding the blessing in which Al HaNissim is inserted, he should go back and say it, unless he already said God’s name at the end of the blessing. [In such a situation], it is proper to say Al HaNissim at the end of Shemoneh Esrei, after the blessings are completed, because one is allowed to add unlimited prayers of supplication and thanksgiving at that point. Similarly, one who forgets to recite Al HaNissim in Birkat HaMazone should say it towards the end of the prayer, together with the HaRachaman paragraphs, where one is permitted to add prayers of thanksgiving as much as he likes (Rama 682:1, M.B. 4).

One who begins his meal on Purim but continues eating long into the night must say Al HaNissim in Birkat HaMazone, because the beginning of the meal is the determinant (Sh.A. 695:3; see M.B. 16).

The Rabbis prescribed that three people be called to the Torah on Purim to read the section beginning with Amalek came (Shemot 17:8-16). Now, even though one of Ezra’s enactments was not to read less than ten verses from the Torah [in public], we read nine on Purim because the entire account [of Amalek’s attack on Israel] contains only nine verses. Some have a custom to read the last verse twice in order to arrive at a total of ten verses ( Sh.A. 693:4), while others do not (Rama, ibid.).

This halachah alludes [to an interesting point]: As long as Amalek’s name has yet to be eradicated, HaShem’s name cannot be revealed fully. Therefore, when we read the section of “Amalek came,” we read [the incomplete number of] nine verses.

Even though the Jews experienced a great salvation on Purim, [the Rabbis] did not institute the recitation of Hallel on that day. The Talmud ( Megillah 14a) gives three reasons for this: Rabbi Yitzchak explains that we do not say Hallel on a miracle that occurred outside the Land of Israel. According to Rava, [the reason it is omitted] is because we remained subjugated to Achashveirosh [even after the miracle occurred], and Hallel can be said only over a salvation that brings us freedom. Rav Nachman holds that the reading of the Megillah is considered like Hallel; therefore, there is no need to prescribe the recitation of Hallel.

It is forbidden to deliver eulogies or fast on the fourteenth and fifteenth of Adar, whether one lives in a walled or unwalled city. The only time it is permissible to deliver a eulogy is at the funeral of a Torah scholar, provided that the body is present (Sh.A., O.C. 696:3, Y.D. 401:5).

We omit Tachanun and LaMinatzei’ach from our prayers on both days of Purim (Sh.A. 693:3). We also omit Tachanun from the Minchah service of Ta’anit Esther, if it immediately precedes Purim (M.B. 131:33).

It is customary to wear Shabbat/Yom Tov clothing on the night of Purim (Rama 695:2, K.H.C. 13).

15.15 – Working on Purim

Our Sages did not originally establish Purim as a holiday on which work is forbidden. Over time, however, the Jewish people developed a custom, out of respect for the sanctity of the day, not to work; and the custom is binding. Our Sages go so far as to say that anyone who works on Purim will never see any blessing from it (B.Y., Sh.A., Rama 696:1).

Therefore, one is forbidden to go to his regular workplace on Purim. If, however, refraining from work would cause him or his employer great loss, he is allowed to work on Purim. It is also permissible for a pauper who has nothing to eat to work on Purim (Sha’ar HaTziyun 696:2-3).

It is permissible to do joyous work on Purim, like preparing an apartment for a soon-to-be-married son and planting decorative trees in one’s yard. One is also permitted to do mitzvah-related work, like writing down novel Torah insights. It is also permissible to do light work, like writing a letter. All of this is allowed provided that it does not ruin ones joy or prevent one from performing the mitzvot of Purim (Sh.A. 696:1, M.B. 6).

Cutting nails is permitted on Purim, because it is a light task, while washing clothes by hand, sewing, and haircutting are forbidden (Ben Ish Chai, TeTzaveh 21). However, one is permitted to do larger tasks if they are needed for Purim, including washing clothes by hand, sewing, and haircutting (Rama 696:1).

According to the letter of the law, one is allowed to engage in commerce on Purim, because successful transactions can bring one joy. However, it is fitting to act strictly in this matter, because such endeavors can drag on and take away from the joy of Purim. Therefore, it is proper to open only those stores that sell Purim necessities (M.B. 696:3, A.H.S. 2, K.H.C. 5).

The custom of refraining from work on Purim takes effect only during the day. Granted, some Acharonim rule strictly and forbid work at night, as well. But the very fact that there is a debate regarding nighttime shows clearly that there is no accepted custom forbidding work at night. Therefore, one may work on the night of Purim (see B.H. 696:1).

Inhabitants of unwalled cities may work on Shushan Purim, and those of walled cities may work on the first day of Purim (Sh.A. 696:2).

However, one who does not know how to vocalize [the words] properly does more harm than good [by reading from his own Megillah]. After all, we already learned that a mistake that affects the meaning of a word invalidates the reading. The Shulchan Aruch (690:4) and Mishnah Berurah (13) explain why a listener should not read [along with the reader] from a printed Megillah.

[14] One who comes late to the synagogue and finds that the congregation has already begun reading the Megillah should not say, “I’ll listen to the Megillah and make up the first few verses afterwards,” for that constitutes reading it backwards. Rather, if he fears that he will not be able to hear it again later, he should quickly recite the blessings, begin reading from a printed Megillah until he catches up to the reader, and hear the rest from him. This will work only if he hears most of the Megillah from the reader. If, however, he fears that by the time he finds the formulation of the blessings [and recites them], the reader will advance to such an extent that he will not be able to catch up to him before midway through the Megillah – and he will not be able to fulfill the mitzvah any other way – he is better off skipping the blessings. This way, he will have enough time to catch up to the reader before the half-way mark and fulfill the mitzvah (see B.H. 690:3, s.v. ve’davka).

[15] See Peninei Halachah, Berachot 12:10, [where we cited] a dispute among the poskim regarding this issue. Rav Kook ( Orach Mishpat 48) and Rav Frank (Mikra’ei Kodesh 11) maintain that one discharges his obligation [by way of an electronic device]. [Rav Moshe Feinstein] also tends towards this viewpoint in Iggrot Moshe (O.C. vol. 2, 108 and vol. 4, 91:4). On the other hand, the authors of Mishpatei Uziel, Minchat Shlomo (1:9), and Yechaveh Da’at (3:54) hold that one does not fulfill his obligation [in this way]. Le’chatchillah, one should avoid using electronic devices to discharge his obligation, but if there is no choice, one can rely on the more lenient authorities and fulfill the mitzvah at least according to their opinion.

[16] The Rishonim debate whether or not mitzvot require intent. The Shulchan Aruch (O.C. 60:4, 690:13-14) settles the matter and rules that one must perform a mitzvah with intent to fulfill God’s command. Many Acharonim hold that this applies to Rabbinic mitzvot, as well, [meaning that] one does not discharge his obligation without intent. Ideally, one should have this intent in mind explicitly, but even if he does not, he is still considered to have performed the mitzvah with intent if he would answer the question “Why did you just read the Megillah?” by saying, “To fulfill the mitzvah,” as the Mishnah Berurah (60:10) explains. Therefore, anyone who comes to synagogue for Megillah reading or recites a blessing before reading the Megillah clearly has intention to fulfill the mitzvah and discharges his obligation. Only someone who sits at home and hears the reading from a synagogue fails to discharge his obligation, unless he explicitly intends to fulfill the mitzvah.

[17] In contrast to the blessings we recite before the reading, HaRav et Riveinu is not obligatory. Rather, it is a blessing of praise and thanksgiving, which depends on custom, as the Mishnah states, “In a place where the custom is to recite the blessing, one should recite it, [and in a place where the custom is] not to recite the blessing, one should not recite it” (Megillah 21a). Nowadays, everyone recites it. The author of Orchot Chayim quotes the Yerushalmi as saying that one recites it only in a minyan, and the Beit Yosef and Rama codify this in 692:1. However, Rashi, the Maharam, and the Radvaz hold that even an individual recites it. The Ben Ish Chai (TeTzaveh 13) concurs, and those who follow his decisions act accordingly. The authors of Bi’ur Halachah (692:2) and Yabi’a Omer (O.C. 8:56) write that one should not recite the blessing [unless a minyan is present], because the issue is unsettled. (This [decision] is especially [logical] seeing that the Behag and Mahari Weill hold that an individual omits even the first [three] blessings.) The prevalent custom is not to say it [when reading the Megillah] alone, but one should not rebuke those who want to recite it. It is unclear whether women count towards the ten (Rama 690:18). The Pri Chadash and Pri Megadim write that this uncertainty relates only to [the question of whether women can be counted] together with men, but a minyan [of ten] women [certainly] recites the blessing. Although some have a custom not to say it, the halachah follows [those who maintain] that it should be said in [a group of] ten women, as the authors of Mikra’ei Kodesh (Frank, Purim 35) and Yabi’a Omer (vol. 8, O.C. 56:4) write. See also Piskei Teshuvot 692:4. It seems that, in practice, women can also be counted together with men, be’di’avad, since many [authorities] hold that even one person may recite this blessing. See Torat HaMo’adim 5:14 and Hilchot Chag BeChag 1-4.

[19] The laws of Al HaNissim in the Amidah prayer are the same for Purim and Chanukah, as the Shulchan Aruch and Rama elucidate (693:2 and 682:1). We elaborated above, 11:8. There is, however, a difference when it comes to Birkat HaMazone. On Chanukah, reciting Al HaNissim in Birkat HaMazone is based on custom, while reciting it on Purim is obligatory. Some claim that one who forgets to say Al HaNissim at the Purim meal, during the day, must repeat Birkat HaMazone, for one is obligated to eat the Purim meal over bread (Maharshal, Shelah, Taz). Others hold that one need not repeat the prayer, because there is no obligation to eat bread at the meal (Terumat HaDeshen, Magen Avraham, Eliyah Rabbah). Still others maintain that even though one must eat bread at the meal, the law of Al HaNissim at the meal is no more stringent than that of Al HaNissim in Shemoneh Esrei, for which one need not repeat the prayer (Aruch HaShulchan 695:7, 12). The Mishnah Berurah (695:15) concludes that one should not repeat Birkat HaMazone because we [generally] rule leniently in cases of doubt regarding blessings.
