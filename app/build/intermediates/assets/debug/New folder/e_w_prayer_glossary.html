Glossary

 

Aĥaronim

Halakhic authorities from c. 1500 CE until the present day

Ahava Rabba

"Abundant Love"; the second of the morning Birkhot Keri’at Shema according to Ashkenazic custom, recited immediately prior to Shema. It praises God for electing Israel and giving them the Torah

Ahavat Olam

"Eternal Love"; the second of the evening Birkhot Keri’at Shema as well as the second of the morning Birkhot Keri’at Shema according to Sephardic custom. It is recited immediately prior to Shema and praises God for electing Israel and giving them the Torah

Al Ha-nisim

"For the Miracles"; a paragraph added to the Amida and Birkat Hamazon on Ĥanuka and Purim

Al Netilat Yadayim

"Concerning the Washing of the Hands"; theberakha recited upon performing Netilat Yadayim

Aleinu

"It is our Duty"; the prayer recited at the end of each of the three daily services

aliya

the calling of a congregant up to the Torah scroll as a section of it is read aloud

ama (pl. amot)

a cubit; a standard halakhic measure of distance equaling c. 45cm and approximating the distance from the tip of the middle finger to the elbow of the average adult male

Amida

"The Standing Prayer"; also called the Shemoneh Esrei; the central prayer of each service, in which the worshipper stands as though in God's presence

amud ha-shaĥar

dawn

Aneinu

"Answer Us"; a paragraph added to the Amida on fast days

aron kodesh

the ark - an ornamental closet that holds each synagogue's Torah scrolls

Asher Yatzar

"Who has Formed"; a berakha recited after using the bathroom

Ashkenaz

a geographical region corresponding to modern-day Germany where the traditions that eventually became characteristic of European (Ashkenazic) Jewry coalesced in the Middle Ages

Ashrei

"Fortunate"; a psalm (Tehilim 145 plus several added verses) that is recited thrice daily in Jewish prayers, twice during Shaĥarit and once during Minĥa.

askmakhta

"Support" for a rabbinic enactment from the text of the Torah

Ata Ĥonen

"You have Graced"; the fourth berakha of the weekday Amida, asking God to grant wisdom to humanity

avoda (she-ba-lev)

"service/worship (of the heart)"; a term for prayer

Avot

"Forefathers"; the first berakha of the Amida, praising God as our God and the God of our ancestors

bal teshaktzu

"do not abominate yourself"; a general mitzva that includes the prohibition on praying while urgently needing to use the bathroom

Barkhu

"Bless"; the call to worship recited at various points in the prayer service and which requires a minyan

Barukh She-amar

"Blessed is He who Spoke"; the opening berakha of Pesukei De-zimra

Barukh Shem

"Blessed is the Name"; the second line Shema and a rabbinic addition to the biblical text; recited silently except on Yom Kippur

batei midrash

Torah study halls

be-di’avad

a level of performance that ex post facto satisfies an obligation in a less-than-ideal manner

beit din

rabbinical court

berakha (pl. berakhot)

a formal blessing recited before eating or performing a mitzva, and on other occasions

berakha le-vatala

a blessing recited in vain

Birkat Ha’avoda

the third to last berakha of the Amida, praising God for the eventual restoration of His presence and the sacrificial rites to the Temple in Jerusalem

Birkat Ha-levana

a berakha recited each month as the moon waxes

Birkat Ha-mazon

known as the "grace after meals"; theberakha aĥarona consisting of four berakhot recited after a bread-based meal

Birkat Ha-minim

the 12th berakha of the weekday Amida and the one added at a later date, it petitions God to eliminate enemies of the Jewish people

Birkat Ha-shanim

the 9th berakha of the weekday Amida, it petitions God for sufficient livelihood

Birkat Kohanim

the three verses (Bamidbar 6:23-25) by which the Kohanim channel God's blessing to the Jewish people

Birkhot Ha-nehenin

berakhot recited just before pleasurable experiences like eating food or smelling fragrances

Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

a series of berakhot recited each morning, praising God for meeting our most basic needs

Birkhot Ha-Torah

the berakhot recited prior to studying Torah, traditionally recited each morning

Birkhot Keri’at Shema

the set of three or four berakhot recited before and after Shema at Shaĥarit and Ma'ariv

Borei Nefashot

the shortest berakha aĥarona, recited after eating a kezayit of food other than grains or the special fruits of Eretz Yisrael, or a revi'it of drink other than wine or grape juice

brit mila

the ritual circumcision (mila) performed on the eighth day of a Jewish boy's like, when he enters into Israel's covenant (brit) with God

de-Oraita

biblically mandated

devarim she-bikdusha

"sacred words" or "sacred matters" that must be treated or recited with a greater degree of respect

Ein Ke-Elokeinu

"There is None like our God”; a liturgcal poem that introduces Pitum Ha-ketoret

Elokai Neshama

"My God, the Soul"; a berakha recited each morning thanking God for restoring our sould to us

Elokai Netzor

"My God, Guard"; a paragraph added at the end of the Amida asking God to help us treat others and help others treat us properly

Emet Ve-emuna

"True and Faithful"; the opening words of the berakha of redemption recited immediately following Shema during Ma'ariv

Emet Ve-yatziv

"True and Upstanding"; the opening words of the berakha of redemption recited immediately following Shema during Shaĥarit

Eretz Yisrael

the Land of Israel

erva

"nakedness"; a general term for illicit sexuality

Full-Kaddish

The “Mourner's Kaddish" or " Kaddish Yatom" said at specific points in all prayer services, generally by congregants who lost a parent within the past year or who are observing a yahrzeit

Ga’al Yisrael

"Who Redeemed Israel"; the berakha of redemption recited after Shema of Shaĥarit and Ma'ariv

gabbai

the congregant in charge of running services in the synagogue, selecting prayer leaders, and calling people up to the Torah

Ge’onim (sing. Gaon)

the leaders of the Babylonian yeshivot and authoritative interpreters of the Bavli during the latter part of the first millennium CE

Gemara

the part of the Talmud that interprets and expands upon the Mishna; compiled during the 3rd-6th centuries CE

hafrashat ĥalla

allocating ĥalla

haftara

a selection from the books of Nevi’im (Prophets) that is publicly read in synagogues on Shabbat, festivals, and fast days

Ha-gafen

the berakha recited over wine and grape juice

hagbaha

the ritual lifting of the Torah for the congregation to see

Ha-Kel Ha-Kadosh

The third berakha of the Amida, praising God for His holiness

hak'hel

the assembly at the Temple at the end of the Shemita year, at which the king would read part of the Torah to all Israel

halakha

the collective body of Jewish law; an individual Jewish law

Half-Kaddish

The Kaddish used to punctuate divisions and transitions within the prayer service

ĥalitza

levirate divorce; the shoe-removal ceremony by which a widow severs her connection to her late and childless husband's brother

ĥalla

the mitzva to give a part of a large batch of dough to a kohen (nowadays it is burnt instead)

Hallel

chapters 113-118 of Tehilim, all of which are thanksgiving psalms, recited on Jewish holidays

Halleluyot

the five psalms of praise (Tehilim 146-150) recited as part of Pesukei De-zimra, each of which begins and ends with the word "halleluyah" ("praise God")

Ha-ma’ariv Aravim

"Who Makes Evening"; the first of the Birkhot Keri’at Shema of Ma'ariv, praising God for creating day and night

Ha-ma’avir Sheina

"Who Removes Sleep"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar, praising God for removing slumber from our eyes

Ha-mapil

"Who Causes Sleep"; part of the bedtime Shema, a berakha praising God for bringing slumber to our eyes

Ha-mekhin Mitzadei Gaver

"Who Prepares a Person’s Strides"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

hanetz ha-ĥama

sunrise

Ha-noten La-sekhvi Vina

"Who Gives the Rooster Understanding"; the first of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

Hashiva Shofteinu

"Restore our Judges"; the 11th berakha of the weekday Amida, asking God to restore the righteous justice system of yore

Hashkiveinu

the second of the Birkhot Keri’at Shema of Ma'ariv in which we pray that God protect us as we sleep

hatarat nedarim

the annulment of vows before a tribunal of three men, generally performed around Rosh Hashana

hatzdakat ha-din

the acceptance of God's justice in the face of seeming unfairness

ĥatzot

midday or midnight, calculated as the midpoint between sunrise and sunset

Havdala

The series of berakhot that marks the end of Shabbat and festivals

ĥazan

The person leading the congregation in prayer

Ĥazarat Ha-shatz

the ĥazan's repetition of the Amida aloud

hefsek

a problematic interruption between two acts or recitations that must be performed or recited together

ĥesed

"loving-kindness"; one of God's attributes that we are enjoined to emulate
 

the intermediate days of Sukkot and Pesaĥ, on which certain weekday activities are permitted

ĥumash

the Pentateuch; a term for Torah in printed form

ĥutz la-aretz

countries outside of Eretz Yisrael

Kabbala

esoteric Jewish wisdom

Kabbalat Shabbat

the series of psalms and poems developed by the kabbalists of Tzefat (Safed) in the 16th century to usher in Shabbat on Friday night

Kaddish

a hymn of praises to God whose central theme is the magnification and sanctification of God’s name

Kaddish De-rabanan

the Kaddish recited after a part of the service that includes extracts from rabbinic works

Kaddish Titkabel

the Kaddish that follows the Amida and marks the end of the main part of the service

kavana

concentration, intention, or mindfulness; the ideal state of mind during prayer and the performance of mitzvot

Kedusha

a responsive prayer of three verses recited in the third berakha of Ĥazarat Ha-shatz , praising God as the ministering angels do and therefore requiring a minyan

Kedusha De-sidra

the three verses of Kedusha recited together with their Aramaic translations at the end of Shaĥarit of weekdays and at Minĥa on Shabbat

ketuba

the traditional Jewish prenuptial contract

kezayit (pl. kezeytim)

an olive’s bulk, a standard halakhic measure of volume or weight; see sections 16:23-24

Kibbutz Galuyot

the 10th berakha of the weekday Amida, asking God to gather all of Israel's exiles

kiddush

the invocation of the sanctity of a holy day with berakhot over a cup of wine

kiddushin

sanctification or dedication, also called erusin (betrothal), the first of the two stages of the Jewish wedding process

kohen (pl. kohanim)

a Jewish priest, descendant of Aaron, charged with performing the Temple rites and benefitting from certain privileges

korban (pl. korbanot)

a sacrificial offering

Korbanot

the part of the prayer service in which paragraphs about the Temple korbanot are recited

La-menatze’aĥ

A psalm (Tehilim 20) recited toward the end of the weekday Shaĥarit

leĥem mishneh

Two loaves of bread at Shabbat and Yom Tov meals in commemoration of the double portion of manna that fell before Shabbat and Yom Tov

Lekha Dodi

A poem composed by R. Shlomo Alkabetz that is part of Kabbalat Shabbat

le-khatĥila

ab initio ; a level of performance that satisfies an obligation in an ideal manner

lo ta’aseh

negative commandments (prohibitions)

lulav

a closed palm frond, one of the four species used during the holiday of Sukkot

Ma’ariv

evening prayers

ma'aser (pl. ma'asrot)

the tithe of one tenth of produce, given to Levites

Magen Avot

A paragraph that summarizes the sevenberakhot of the Friday night Amida

maĥatzit ha-shekel

the half-shekel per capita that funded public offerings in the Temple, and which is given symbolically nowadays

maĥzor (pl. maĥzorim)

the prayer book used on festivals

Malbish Arumim

"Who Clothes the Naked"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

mara d’atra

"master of the house" in Aramaic, the local authority in Jewish law

matanot le-evyonim

a prophetic precept to give at least two gifts to two poor people on Purim

Matir Asurim

"Who Releases the Imprisoned"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

Me-ein Shalosh

The blessing said after eating a kezayit of grain or of the special fruits of Eretz Yisrael, or after drinking a revi'it of wine or grape juice

Megilat Esther

The Book or "Scroll" of Esther, read aloud on Purim

Meĥayei Ha-metim

"Who Resurrects the Dead"; the second berakha of the Amida, praising God's omnipotence

Men of the Great Assembly

a group of sages and prophets from the beginning of the Second Temple era (c. 500 BCE) who instituted several enactments that shaped the course of halakha and Jewish tradition

mezuza

the doorpost, or the parchment inscribed with specific paragraphs from the Torah that must be affixed to the doorpost of Jewish homes

mikveh

a ritual immersion pool

tashlumim

the afternoon prayers

minhag

an custom or traditional practice, or a set of customs or traditional practices

minyan

a quorum of ten adult Jewish males required for certain religious obligations

Mishkan

Tabernacle; the portable dwelling place for the Divine Presence that was the center of Israelite worship before the Temple was constructed

mishlo’aĥ manot

a gift of two dishes sent to a friend on Purim day

Mishna

The earliest authoritative work of rabbinic literature consisting of legal statements and disputes arranged in 63 tractates and 6 orders, compiled in the 3rd century CE

Mizmor Le-David

Tehilim 29, recited as part of Kabbalat Shabbat and as the Torah scroll is returned to the aron ha-kodesh on Shabbat

Mizmor Le-toda

Tehilim 100, recited as part of Pesukei De-zimra except on Shabbat, festivals, Ĥol Ha-mo’ed of Pesaĥ, and the day before Yom Kippur and Pesaĥ

Moda (Modeh) Ani

a short prayer recited daily upon waking up, while still in bed

Modim

the berakha of thanksgiving in which one bows at the beginning and end; the second to last berakha of the Amida

Motza’ei Shabbat

Saturday night after Shabbat ends and weekday activities are resumed

Mourner’s Kaddish

see Full Kaddish

Musaf

the additional service recited on Shabbat, Yom Tov, Ĥol Ha-mo’ed, and Rosh Ĥodesh, days on which the korban musaf was offered in Temple times

Nefilat Apayim

the part of Taĥanun in which one "falls on one's face" in prayer by crouching and resting one's head on his arm

Ne'ila

the "closing" of the gates; the last of the five Yom Kippur services

netilat yadayim

ritual hand washing

nida

a woman who has menstruated and not yet immersed in a mikveh to purify herself

nusaĥ

a particular prayer rite, defined by a unique wording, tune, order, or practice

omer

a biblical measure of grain and an offering brought in the Temple on the second day of Pesaĥ; the count from that day until Shavu'ot is known as the counting of the omer, or Sefirat Ha-omer

Oral Torah

the traditions and rulings that were transmitted from Sinai but not made explicit in the Written Torah

Oseh shalom

"May He Who makes peace"; the passage that commonly concludes Jewish prayers

Oter Yisrael Be-tifara

"Who Crowns Israel with Glory"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

Ozer Yisrael Bi-gevura

"Who Girds Israel with Strength"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

Parshat Zakhor

the Torah passage read on the Shabbat before Purim to fulfill the mitzva of remembering what Amalek did to Israel

Pesukei De-zimra

the psalms of praise recited prior toShaĥarit which prepares one for the Amida

pidyon ha-ben

a mitzva in which a Jewish firstborn son is redeemed from a kohen with five silver coins

Pitum Ha-ketoret

the paragraphs recited as part of the prayer service that pertain to the incense that was offered in the Temple

Poke’aĥ Ivrim

"Who Gives Sight to the Blind"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

Posek (pl. poskim)

A halakhic decisor or authority

rabbanit

the wife of a rabbi who is a religious figure in her own right

rav muvhak

one's primary teacher

Refa’enu

the 9th berakha of the weekday Amida, in which we pray for health and wellbeing

Responsa

A genre of rabbinic literature that consists of rabbinic responses to halakhic queries

revi'it

a talmudic liquid measure equal to c. 75ml or a "quarter" of a log

Rishonim

Jewish sages and halakhic authorities from the medieval era (roughly 1000-1500 CE)

Rosh Ĥodesh

the new moon; the one or two-day minor holiday that marks the beginning of each Hebrew month

ru’aĥ ra’ah

an "evil spirit" that contaminates people in certain circumstances until he washes his hands

safek

a case of uncertainty or doubt

safek de-rabanan

an uncertainly about a rabbinic ordinance

Sanhedrin

the Jewish high court of 71 judges that sat on the Temple Mount

Savora'im

the Sages after the Amora'im but before the Ge'onim who were responsible for much of the Bavli's editing

Selaĥ Lanu

"Forgive Us"; the sixth berakha of the weekday Amida, in which one asks God for forgiveness

Sepharad

Iberia, where the customs of Jews who lived mainly under Muslim rule coalesced into what is now known as Sephardic custom

sha’ot zemaniyot

seasonal hours, calculated by dividing the daylight into twelve equal segments

Shaĥarit

the morning prayers

Shamor

the negative aspects of Shabbat observance, namely, the requirement to refrain from certain activities

She-asani ki-rtzono

"Who Made me According to His Will"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar, recited by women in lieu of She-lo Asani Isha

She-asa li Kol Tzorki

"Who has Provided me with all my Needs"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

She-hakol

the generic berakha on food, recited on foods not included in the purview of any other berakha

She-heĥeyanu

a common blessing said to celebrate special occasions, it is recited to be thankful for new and unusual experiences

Shekhina

the divine presence in this world

She-lo Asani Aved

"Who has not Made me a Slave"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

She-lo Asani Goy

"Who has not Made me a Gentile"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

She-lo Asani Isha

"Who has not Made me a Woman"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar

shem u-malkhut

the invocation of God's name and kingship that are key components of every formal berakha

Shema

the three Torah paragraphs (or the first of the three paragraphs) whose recitation is a centerpiece of the morning and evening prayers; its opening verse is the Jewish credo: "Hear, O Israel: the Lord is our God, the Lord is one"

Shemini Atzeret

The independent holiday celebrated on what would be the eighth day of Sukkot

Shemita

every seventh year, when Jews must not work their fields in Eretz Yisrael

Shemoneh Esrei

"The Eighteen," (in reference to the original number of constituent blessings; there are now nineteen); the Amida

Shir shel Yom

the "song of the day" that the Levites would sing in the Temple and which is now recited at the end of Shaĥarit

Shirat Ha-yam

the "Song of the Sea" sung by Moshe and the Israelites after the splitting of the Red Sea, now recited daily as part of Pesukei De-zimra

shofar

a musical instrument made of a horn, traditionally that of a ram, blown as part of the Rosh Hashana ritual as well as other rituals

Shomei’a Tefila

"Who Heard Prayer"; the 16th berakha of the weekday Amida in which we ask God to answer our prayers and may insert personal petitions

siddur

a Jewish prayer book

sukka

a temporary hut constructed for use during the weeklong festival of Sukkot

Taĥanun

"Supplication"; the heartbreaking prayers recited after the Amida of Shaĥarit and Minĥa, omitted on festive occasions

Tamid

the paragraph recited as part of Korbanot that refers to the twice-daily tamid burnt-offering brought in the Temple

tanna kama

the anonymous first opinion cited in a mishna or beraita

Tanna’im

The rabbinic authorities in Eretz Yisrael during the first two centuries CE

tashlumim

the make-up prayer recited by one who accidentally missed one prayer

tefaĥ

a handbreadth; a halakhic measurement equal to c. 8cm

tefilat nedava

a voluntary prayer; an extraShmoneh Esrei in addition toShaĥarit, Minĥa, and Maariv

tefillin

phylacteries; black leather boxes and straps containing parchment scrolls, worn by men during weekday Shaĥarit

Tehilla Le-David

Psalm 145; see Ashrei

teruma (pl. terumot)

a tithe of c. 2% of produce, given to kohanim

Thirteen Attributes

the merciful qualities with which God governs the world, based on Shemot 34:6-7

tikun (olam)

"rectification"; an action designed to bring the world closer to its ultimate wholeness

tokh kedei dibur

within the amount of time it takes to utter a short phrase (e.g. "Shalom alekha rabbi")

Torah reading

the public reading of a set of passages from a Torah scroll

tosefet Shabbat

the time added before and/or after Shabbat to fulfill the mitzva of extending Shabbat into the week

tum’a

ritual contamination/impurity

tzitzit

four-cornered tasseled garments worn on a tallit in fulfillment of a mitzva

tzni’ut

modesty, humility, or privacy, a character trait that governs Jewish behavior and dress, based on the notion that one is always in God's presence

vatikin

the "early saints" who would pray at sunrise

Va-yekhulu

the Torah verses that first invoke Shabbat (Bereishit 2:1-3), typically recited three times on Friday night

Va-yevarekh David

a paragraph in Pesukei De-zimra that recounts how King David blessed God in the presence of the entire congregation at the public coronation of his son and successor, Shlomo

Va-yomer

the third paragraph of Shema, which mentions the Exodus and the mitzva of tzitzit

Ve-haya im Shamo’a

the second paragraph of the Shema, which encapsulates the reward and punishment for performing and shirking mitzvot

Ve-hu Raĥum

the expanded Taĥanun recited on Monday and Thursday

Vi-yhi No’am

part of the bedtime Shema (and other prayers) that begins with Tehilim 90:17 and includes all of Tehilim 91

Ya’aleh Ve-yavo

the paragraph inserted into Shemoneh Esrei and Birkat Ha-mazon of festivals on which Musaf

Yehi Khevod

a collection of twenty-one verses taken primarily from Psalms and recited as part of Pesukei De-zimra

Yihyu le-ratzon

the verse (Tehilim 19:15) recited at the end of the Amida marking the formal end of the prayer

Yishtabaĥ

the concluding berakha of Pesukei De-zimra

Yotzer Or

"Who Forms Light"; the first of the Birkhot Keri’at Shema of Shaĥarit , in which God is praised as creator of the celestial beings

Zakhor

the positive aspects of Shabbat observance, namely, the requirement to mark it in specific ways ,such as by reciting kiddush

Zokef Kefufim

"Who Straightens the Bent"; one of the Birkhot Ha-shaĥar
