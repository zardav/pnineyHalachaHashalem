Editor's Introduction

The commandment to learn and study the words of the Torah is the most important mitzvah given to the Jewish people (Yerushalmi Peah 1:1;Bavli Moed Katan 9b). This mitzvah obligates us to understand all the fundamentals of the Torah. This is explained byShulchan Aruch Harav (Hilchot Talmud Torah 1:5-7) and summarized in Peninei Halachah (Likutim I, Hilchot Talmud Torah 3): "The mitzvah to learn Torah requires every Jew to know all the fundamental principles of the Torah so that he will know how to act and how to relate to the events of his life according to the Torah's guidance. Indeed, in essence, the Torah knows no bounds. From every letter in the Torah it is possible to learn numerous laws and allusions, and in every rabbinic Midrash countless ideas and explanations are concealed. As is written (Job 11:9) 'Its measure is longer than the earth and wider than the sea.' For the root of the Torah lies in eternity, and it is therefore clear that there is no limit to the profundity of its ideas. Nevertheless, to the fundamental Torah principles, meaning the written Torah, the halachah (law), and its reasons, there are boundaries; it is possible to learn and grasp them."

For one learning Torah today, at first glance, this definition seems difficult to comprehend. Is it truly possible for one to know all the fundamental principles of the Torah? Hundreds of years ago, when people were engrossed in learning mainly the Bible, Mishnah, and the Talmud, it was possible to fulfill this mitzvah, but today, the Torah has become an infinite and endless sea of ideas and details. The following verses of the Torah (Deuteronomy 30:11-14), "This mandate that I am prescribing to you today is not too mysterious or remote from you. It is not in heaven... It is something that is very close to you. It is in your mouth and in your heart so that you can keep it" seem to refer to aspirations for the future.

Indeed opinions and commentaries on the Torah have multiplied exponentially. The Torah has expanded so much that a person who wishes to truly study a certain topic to its fullest is required to learn from many different books, study tens of Rishonim and Acharonim and then read various responsa in order to comprehend and contend with the halachic questions which arise from that topic. As a result, in recent generations, an array of halachic books have been written which provide detailed outlines and summarize an entire topic from all its aspects. Each book provides its own advantage; some are expansive and thoroughly assemble all that is written on the topic, others organize their commentary according to the order of the Shulchan Aruch and some are based on meticulously expounding upon the words of the prominent Acharonim.

However, the main objective of the commandment to learn Torah - learning all of the fundamentals of the Torah - has become seemingly distant and far from reach. The majority of halachic writings today do not emphasize clarifying the principles and general rules that arise from a topic and when a person learns from these books alone, the halachic topic may become difficult to grasp. The extensive details seem never-ending and the task of memorizing tremendous amounts of minutiae within the halachah seems daunting and almost impossible.

Elucidating the principles and providing halachic clarity are the primary goals of my rabbi and teacher Hagaon HaRav Eliezer Melamed, shlita, in his series of books, Peninei Halachah. The aim of this series is to allow the mitzvah of Torah learning to be fulfilled by all and make Torah knowledge accessible to every Jew. Each halachah is summarized and concisely reviewed from its source, along with the rationale behind it, so that one may review the topic quickly and still encompass the fundamentals of Torah within a short time. Moreover, the books were written to accommodate people on all different levels of learning; a person who is not familiar with a topic or halachah can easily understand the books' clear language and grasp the practical halachah. At the same time, even one who has learned the Rishonim and Acharonim on a topic will find the concise halachic summary and precise review presented in Peninei Halachah enlightening.

Peninei Halachah is also unique in its approach to halachic rulings from different ethnic groups. In places where the Ashkenazic, Sephardic and Yemenite customs differ, the halachah is detailed separately, but in the overall halachic calculation, poskim from all the different ethnic groups are taken into consideration. As Rav Melamed explains in the introduction to Peninei Halachah - Berachot, "Although in the past, due to the distance between the Diaspora communities, the rabbis took into consideration primarily the rabbis who lived in their surrounding areas, in their generation or in the generations before them; today when we live and learn together among the different tribes of Israel, all the poskim from all the ethnic groups must be taken into consideration."

Baruch Hashem, the Peninei Halachah series is being learned by people all over Israel and gaining popularity daily. The books' unique and captivating method of teaching halachah has garnered praise and admiration and currently, with God's help, over 200,000 Hebrew copies have been sold. With the aim to make Peninei Halachah accessible to more people, it is currently being translated into other languages, among them, French, Czech and Braille. This book is the first of the series to be translated into English.

The translation of this book was no small feat. As its name suggests, Peninei Halachah is a halachic work, which necessitates a particularly meticulous and accurate translation so that no mistake would be made regarding the practical halachah. At the same time, in Peninei Halachah an emphasis is put on a concise synopsis as well as lucidity and flowing language. Much care went into trying to preserve the succinct yet eloquent language without sacrificing precision in halachic detail. We hope that we succeeded in providing those who learn this book with the best possible product, although it is inevitable that in every translation some of the character and language of the original is diminished or lost. Any and all mistakes in translation are the full responsibility of the editor alone and not the author.

פתיחה למהדורה האנגלית

מצווה מיוחדת נצטווה עם ישראל לקראת כניסתו לארץ, שביום עוברו את הירדן יקים אבנים גדולות ויעשה מהם מזבח, ויכתוב עליהם את כל דברי התורה באר היטב (דברים כז, א-ח). ואמרו חכמים שהכוונה לכתוב עליהם את התורה בשבעים לשון (סוטה לב, א). בעוד אנו בחוץ לארץ עיקר מאמצנו מופנים להישרדות, כדי שלא נשכח מהיכן יצאנו ולהיכן נשוב. אולם בארץ ישראל אנו צריכים לזכור שתפקידנו הוא להאיר את העולם כולו, ולכן עלינו לכתוב את התורה בשבעים לשון, כדי שתתבאר היטב בכל הבחינות ותאיר לכל העמים. יהי רצון שתרגום "פניני הלכה" יהיה נדבך בגילוי הארת התורה לעולם.

בראש ובראשונה ספר זה מוקדש לכל היהודים הצדיקים שוויתרו על מגוריהם בארצות השפע, על מעמד וממון, ועלו לארץ זבת חלב ודבש, ארץ התורה והנבואה, ארץ הקודש והמקדש, ארץ אשר אבניה ברזל, ומלחמות קשות, מפנים ומאחור, נדרשות כדי להתיישב בה. השפה האנגלית היתה שגורה על פיהם, שפה בינלאומית שפתחה לפניהם שערים בכל העולם, ועלו לארץ אבותינו, לדבר בשפתנו הקדושה והעתיקה. מאנשים שידעו להתבטא ברהיטות, בדייקנות וברגש, הפכו לעלגי לשון שצריכים לעיתים את עזרת ילדיהם כדי להסתדר בארץ. ומי יודע אם אנו, שאבותינו נולדו בארץ, היינו יכולים לעלות בהר ה' כמותם. זכות גדולה נפלה בחלקם של המתרגמים 'פניני הלכה' לאנגלית, לסייע לאנשים היקרים והצדיקים הללו ללמוד דברי תורה מתורת ארץ ישראל בנחת ובנעימות, בשפה השגורה על לשונם.

ספר זה גם מוקדש לכל היהודים האהובים שעוד נותרו בגלות, שהננו מתפללים ומקווים לעלייתם לארץ במהרה. תפקיד חשוב נועד ליהודי אמריקה, יהדות זו שגילתה כשרונות עצומים ומגוונים, ובחריצות אין קץ הצליחה לתרום תרומה מכרעת להעלאתה של ארצות הברית למעמדה הנכבד כיום, אם יתקשרו בכל אותם הכשרונות והחריצות למורשת אבות, וליבם ומוחם יהיה נתון ליעוד המיוחד של עמנו, יוכלו להאיר את העולם באור התורה, לרפאו מחוליו ולהחיש את גאולתו. הכשרון העצום שגילו בתחומים השונים, והתרבות העולמית והשפה האנגלית השגורה על פיהם, יהיו כלים חשובים לתיקון עולם. ואותם הצדיקים שעלו לארץ ומדברים עברית במבטא המיוחד, העדין והמתוק שלהם, מבשרים כי הנה קרבה גאולתנו.

צדקה עשה ה' עמי שהרב מאור קיים שליט"א, הגיע לישיבת הר ברכה והוא הולך ומתעלה ומתגדל בתורה עד שכבר נעשה מורה הוראה. אחריו בא לישיבה אחיו שלו, ומתוך כך זכיתי להכיר את הוריהם, ד"ר רפאל וד"ר אביבה, שקיימו את מצוות ישוב הארץ למהדרין. הם הסכימו לוותר על הקליניקה המצליחה שהיתה לד"ר רפאל בניו-יורק ועלו אל העתיד הלא ידוע בארץ. בעזרת ה' במשך הזמן שניהם הסתדרו על הצד היותר טוב, ד"ר אביבה עוסקת ביעוץ וד"ר רפאל הינו רופא מחוזי בירושלים והסביבה. כאן המקום להודות לו על הסיוע שהוא מעניק לי בשאלות הלכתיות הנוגעות לרפואה.

את שבת החתן של הרב מאור עשתה משפחת קיים בהר ברכה שבלב השומרון, בסעודת השבת אמר לי ד"ר רפאל, שהוא מרגיש בארץ ישראל כמו בגן עדן. דקות ספורות לאחר הבדלה תקף מחבל ביריות את בני המשפחה, דלית בתם, שהיתה בהריון ספגה ששה כדורים, בעלה יעקב ספג שלושה כדורים. אחד מבני משפחת הכלה יחד עם אחד מבוגרי הישיבה חיסלו את המחבל. סכנת חיים נשקפה לדלית. כשבאתי לבקרם בבית החולים, שאלתי את ד"ר רפאל, שלא עזב את בית החולים עד לשחרורה של בתו, אם הוא עדיין מרגיש בגן עדן, והוא התעקש שאכן כך, ארץ ישראל היא כמו גן עדן. בחסדי ה' דלית נרפאה לגמרי, העובר נולד בשעה טובה. היא כיום אם לארבעה ומשמשת כרופאה בירושלים.

ועתה הרב מאור קיים נטל על עצמו לערוך את תרגום 'פניני הלכה' לאנגלית. עתירה, אחותו הבכורה, תוך גידול ילדיה הקטנים, עסקה בתרגום הספר. גברת דבורה ריין, אמו של חנן תלמיד ישיבתנו, ור' צבי פישמן ערכו את הספר, לשונית וסגנונית, והרב מאור פיקח על הצד ההלכתי. לכולם הנני מודה מעומק לבי. ויהי רצון שדברי התורה הללו יאירו ויתקיימו בלבבם של הלומדים בספר, ומתוך כך נזכה להתפלל כראוי לפני אבינו שבשמיים, ונזכה לכל הברכות הכתובות בתורה, ונזכה לגאולה שלימה במהרה בימינו.

אליעזר מלמד

Glossary

Acharon (pl. Acharonim) - lit. last one. Acharonim are recent rabbis and poskim who were active from the sixteenth century to the present.

Agadah - lit. telling. The sections in Talmudic literature that do not constitute legal matters. Sometimes called Jewish legend, it includes stories and biblical expositions.

Ahavah Rabbah - lit. abundant love. In Nusach Ashkenaz, the prayer recited before Shema, and the second of the three berachot of Keriat Shema in Shacharit, thanking Hashem for giving us His Torah, and requesting that He enable us to learn His Torah with love. Those praying in Nusach Sephard and Eidot Mizrach recite Ahavat Olam, a similar prayer, with a few minor changes in textual wording. See 10:2 and footnote 2 there; also see 16:1.

Ahavat Olam - see Ahavah Rabbah.

Al Netilat Yadayim - lit. on the lifting of the hands. The blessing recited upon washing one's hands. See 8:1.

Aleinu L'Shabe'ach - lit. it is incumbent upon us to give praise. A part of the liturgy recited at the end of the prayer service, conveying Hashem's omnipotence and our thanksgiving for distinguishing the Jewish people from the other nations. See 23:5 and footnote 6 there.

Aliyah (pl. aliyot) - lit. ascent. The calling up of a member of the congregation to read a portion of the Torah reading. Today, since people are not all well-versed in the cantillation signs and the reading, generally one only recites the berachah on the portion of the Torah reading, and the Torah is then read by a ba'al koreh. See 22:5.

Alot HaShachar - lit. the rise of the morning. Dawn. See Amud HaShachar. See 11:2.

Am ha'aretz - lit. people of the land. Generally used to describe an uneducated person, or one who is unlearned in matters of Torah.

Am Yisrael - the nation of Israel, the Jewish people, descendants of Yaakov Avinu (Jacob our forefather), who was also called Yisrael.

Amah (pl. amot) - cubit, unit of length, equivalent to six tefachim.

Amen - lit. true. A response to benedictions and prayers, the word amen stems from the word emunah, meaning faith, and expresses a person's belief in and acceptance of the recited words. See 19:3.

Amidah - lit. standing. A prayer that constitutes the main section of each of the the three daily prayer services, also known asShemoneh Esrei or Tefillah. It is comprised of nineteen blessings and is recited quietly. See 17:1.

Amora (pl. Amoraim) - lit. speaker or one who says. Amoraim were Talmudic scholars who expounded the teachings of the Oral law. Their legal and agadic discussions and debates comprised the Gemara text.

Amud HaShachar - lit. pillar of the morning. Dawn, also called alot hashachar. Usually indicates the beginning of the halachic morning. Its exact time is the subject of halachic debate. See 11:2.

Annus - lit. forced. This refers to a person who finds himself in circumstances beyond his control which deem him exempt from certain mitzvot or sometimes cause him to sin without his intention. See chapter 1, note 3.

Anshei Knesset HaGedolah - Men of the Great Assembly. The Great Assembly, consisting of 120 members, was a legislative body founded by Ezra the Scribe in the time of the Second Temple. This group of sages received the Torah from the prophets, composed many of the prayers, enacted ordinances to preserve Torah observance, finalized the contents of the Tanach, and established certain holidays and fasts. See 1:5.

Asham - guilt offering. There are different kinds of guilt offerings one of which is brought by a person who is uncertain as to whether or not he committed a sin ( asham talui). Some parts of it are eaten by the Kohen. See also Korban.

Asher Yatzar - lit. Who created. This is the berachah recited upon relieving oneself, in which we thank Hashem for creating us with a properly functioning, healthy body.

Ashkenaz - the Rhineland, the medieval Hebrew name for eastern France, Germany, and Eastern Europe. Jews who came from that area and their descendants are calledAshkenazic Jews, or Ashkenazim. They follow Ashkenazic customs. See also Nusach. See chapter 6.

Ashrei - lit. praiseworthy. A prayer recited three times daily (twice in Shacharit and once in Minchah), whose content is mostly comprised of Psalm 145, as well as excerpts from Psalms 84, 144, and 115. See 14:2-3 and footnote 5 there.

Asmachta - support, intimation. Usually a biblical reference brought as confirmation for a rabbinic law or custom.

Asu siyag - lit. created a fence. A term used to describe the establishment of additional regulations instituted by the Chachamim, designed to preserve biblical laws.

Attah Chonantanu - You have graced us. A prayer added in the fourth berachah of the Shemoneh Esrei (Attah Chonen) in Ma'ariv of Motza'ei Shabbat or Motza'ei chag as havdalah, to distinguish between the weekdays and Shabbat or festivals. See 18:2.

Avot - the forefathers. Referring to the founding fathers of Judaism - Avraham (Abraham), Yitzchak (Isaac), and Ya'akov (Jacob), three generations from which came forth the nation of Israel. See 1:2.

B'dieved - ex post facto. A term used to describe a situation in which one fulfills a mitzvah in a less than ideal manner.

Ba'al koreh - lit. master of the reading. The person assigned to read the Torah scroll with cantillation signs for the congregation. See aliyah . Also see 22:5.

Ba'alei habrit - lit. masters of the covenant. The three people directly involved in the circumcision ceremony - the father of the boy being circumcised, the mohel (the circumciser), and the sandak (the one who holds the boy as he is being circumcised). See 21:8.

Ba'alei Mussar - Jewish moralists who composed books of ethics for the nation.

Ba'alei simchah - lit. masters of happiness. Those celebrating a joyous mitzvah occasion. See 21:8.

Baladi - one of the Yemenite ethnic groups. See nusach. See chapter 6.

Bar mitzvah - lit. capable of the commandment. A thirteen-year-old male who has reached the age of mitzvot and is consequently obligated to observe the commandments. This term also denotes the ceremony conducted on the occasion of the boy's attainment at this age.

Barchu - lit. bless. This is the introduction to the three berachot of Keriat Shema, in which the chazan instructs the congregation to begin blessing, as in to start reciting

Birkot Keriat Shema . See 16:3.

Baruch She'amar - lit. Blessed be He who said. The blessing that opens the passages of Pesukei d'Zimrah, in which we extol Hashem, Who created the world and rules over it. See 14:1, 4.

Bein HaShemashot - twilight, the time after the sun has already set but before tzeit hakochavim (the emergence of stars). The status of this particular time is subject to halachic debate. Laws are determined accordingly. See 25:5-7.

Beit din - lit. house of judgment, courthouse. The words beit din are also used to describe a group of three competent Jewish males who serve as a court in certain situations.

Beit Hillel - lit. House of Hillel. A school of Tannaim founded by Hillel the sage, known for its more lenient teachings, which were accepted as halachah in almost all cases.

Beit midrash (pl. batei midrash) - house of study; the place of Torah learning where biblical and Talmudic discourse and exposition are studied. See 3:3.

Beit Shamai - lit. house of Shamai. A school of Tannaim founded by Shamai the sage, known for his more strict teachings, which in almost all cases were not accepted as halachah.

Berachah (pl. berachot) - a blessing or benediction. A formula of thanksgiving and a means of acknowledging human reliance on God for all things.

Bet-Hei-Bet - also known as Behab, referring to the days of the week, Monday, Thursday, and Monday. The custom of introducing fasts in the months of Cheshvan and Iyar as days of repentance for any possible sins committed during the festivals in the preceding months of Tishrei and Nissan.

Bimah - pulpit. The area in the synagogue on which the chazan stands to lead the prayer service and where the Torah scroll is read. See 3:4.

Birkat Avot - the blessing of the forefathers, the opening blessing of the Amidah which mentions the merit of the forefathers ( avot). See 1:7.

Birkat HaMazon - lit. the blessing upon the food, known as the Grace After Meals. This is the berachah recited upon the conclusion of eating a certain amount of bread. It is comprised of four berachot in which we recall Hashem's constant benevolence, thank Him for giving us sustenance and for giving us the Land of Israel, and Jerusalem in particular.

Birkat Kohanim - the priestly blessing, comprised of three Scriptural verses, and chanted at the end of the repetition of the Amidah prayer by the Kohanim from a platform. In Israel it is performed in synagogues daily, whereas in the Diaspora it is performed only on festivals. See chapter 20.

Birkot HaMitzvot - blessings recited upon the performance of commandments.

Birkot HaNehenin - blessings recited upon deriving pleasure from something.

Birkot HaShachar - morning blessings recited upon waking, thanking Hashem for granting us our capabilities and bestowing us with benefits in this world. See chapter 9.

Birkot HaTorah - Torah blessings. The blessings recited in the morning with Birkot HaShachar as well as immediately before and after public Torah reading, recalling the commandment to engross oneself in Torah and expressing thanks for choosing the nation of Israel and giving us the gift of Torah. See chapter 10.

Birkot Keriat Shema - the Shema blessings, comprised of three blessings in Shacharit and four in Ma'ariv, two before the recital of the Shema and one or two after it, which express praise of Hashem's command over creation, thanks concerning His choice to give the Torah to His beloved Jewish people, a prayer for the redemption of Israel, and for the protection of His nation. See chapter 16.

Brit milah - a Jewish male circumcision performed on the eighth day after birth, also called brit (covenant) or bris. See also ba'alei habrit.

Chachamim - the sages, also called Chazal (chachamim zichronam livrachah - sages, may their memory be blessed). A title given to rabbinic Torah scholars who lived in the times of the Mishnah and Talmud. See 1:6.

Chassidi or Chassidic - see Chassidim and Nusach Chassidi.

Chassidim - lit. devout or pious ones. This term refers both to righteous and devout people who perform extra pious acts and enhance the mitzvot as well to as the followers of the Ultra-Orthodox pious movement founded by Rabbi Israel Ba'al Shem Tov in the first half of the eighteenth century in the Ukraine. See 1:8 and 6:3.

Chatan (pl. chatanim) - groom. See 21:8.

Chatat - sin offering, brought by a person who has sinned against Hashem. There are several different kinds of sin offerings.

Chatzot - the time of day or night at which six proportional hours have passed. Chatzot hayom, halachic noon, is the half-way point between the beginning of the day and the beginning of the night and chatzot halailah, halachic midnight, is the half-way point between the beginning of the night and the beginning of the day. See 11:10-11.

Chazal - see Chachamim.

Chazan (pl. chazanim) - cantor, the person who leads the prayer service. See 4:1-3.

Chazanut - cantillation. The act of being chazan or the reading or chanting of the Torah with cantillation signs. See 4:2 .

Chazarat HaShatz - the repetition of the Amidah recited aloud by the chazan after the silent Amidah during the prayer service. Originally instituted for people who were not well-versed in prayer; the custom of its recital remained. See chapter 19.

Chelbenah - Galbanum, the eleventh spice of the incense. Although by itself it possesses a foul odor, when added to the rest of the components it emits a sweet smell. See 13:6.

Chochmat HaSod - the wisdom of mysticism, a deeper meaning behind the biblical text and the mitzvot. See also Sod.

Dati-Leumi - national-religious. Dati-Leumi is an ideology that combines religious Judaism and Zionism, and is a term used to describe the modern-Orthodox in Israel today. See 6:8.

Duchan - The platform in front of the ark in the synagogue from which the Kohanim bless the congregation. See 20:8.

Eretz Yisrael - the Land of Israel.

Erev - lit. evening. The eve of a Shabbat (Friday night) or a festival, known as Erev Shabbat, Erev Pesach, etc.

Gabbai (pl. gabbaim) - synagogue coordinator, the person who organizes the prayer service and manages synagogue affairs. Tasks may include assigning the aliyot to specific people, granting congregants the honors of opening the ark and carrying the Torah scroll, coordinating seating arrangements, reciting a Mishebeirach, making sure there are enough siddurim, etc.

Gehinnom - Gehenna, hell. Place of punishment in the World to Come.

Gemara - lit. a teaching. Also called Talmud. The Gemara is the interpretation of the Mishnah by the Amoraim contains 63 masechotot or tractates. There are two major compilations of the Talmud: the Talmud Bavli, or Bablylonian Talmud, and the Talmud Yerushalmi, or the Jerusalem Talmud. Aside fromhalachah, the Gemara consists of practical teaching and moral lessons, as well as agadah and stories concerning the sages.

Hagbahah - lit. lifting. The honor of lifting the Torah and displaying its letters to the congregation. See 22:4.

Halachah (pl. halachot) - the word halachah can be used in two ways, to refer to Jewish law in general or to one specific law. When something is in accordance with Jewish law, it is considered halachic.

Hatarat Nedarim - lit. cancellation of vows. A ritual performed by an individual in front of three competent Jewish adult males who, acting as a beit din, can annul a specific vow that he accepted upon himself.

Havdalah - lit. separation or distinction. The havdalah ceremony is a ritual or prayer ceremony marking the end of Shabbat and holidays, and expresses the difference between the sacred day and the remaining secular work days. See 18:2.

Hefsek - lit. stop or pause. An interruption or break between the words of a berachah, prayer, and the performance of the mitzvah. See 4:2, 16: 4-7, and 17:15.

Hirhur k'dibur - thought is like speech. A concept applying to certain laws in which a person fulfills his obligation by thinking the words of a prayer or a blessing and is considered as if he had recited them aloud. See 1:9 and footnote 2 there.

Isru chag - lit. bind the festival. The day after the Shalosh Regalim (three pilgrimage festivals - Pesach, Shavuot, and Sukkot). Regarding certain halachic issues, it is considered a semi-holiday.

K'hora'at sha'ah - lit. as an instruction for the hour. An obligation arranged by the sages for a limited amount of time, designed to resolve a specific problem at hand.

Kabbalah - lit. received. The tradition of mystical teachings. The central text of mysticism is the Zohar HaKadosh, the holy Book of Splendor, which is the mystical commentary on the Torah written by Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, a fifth-generation Tanna who was a pupil of Rabbi Akiva. Kabbalists are those whose writings and teachings are based on the Kabbalah and its ideas.

Kal vachomer - lit. light and heavy. An a fortiori inference. One of the thirteen fundamental principles of biblical exegesis that employs logic to explain a comparison of two situations - one case lenient and the other stringent. The kal vachomer principle maintains that if we are stringent in the normally lenient case, we must certainly be stringent in the stringent case as well.

Kavanah - intent. The directing of one's heart to have the proper intent in one's prayer, blessing, or performance of a mitzvah. Intent is the essence of prayer, the recital of berachot, and the performance of mitzvot. See 1:8, 15:6-8, and 17:9.

Kavanot Ha'Ari - Special intent during certain prayers or berachot according to the mystical traditions handed down by the Ari, Rabbi Isaac Luria.

Kedushat HaMalachim - lit. the holiness of the angels. Refers to a verse in chapter 6 of Isaiah that is chanted by the angels and contains the words "kadosh, kadosh, kadosh." This verse is included in the prayer service twice in Shacharit. See 16:4.

Keter Aram Tzova - lit. the jewel of the crowns. Also called the Aleppo Codex, this Hebrew manuscript of the Torah was considered to be the most reliable of all versions and the authoritative Masoretic text. See 6:2.

Kevod HaBriyot - respect for God's creations, human dignity. This concept is employed to permit certain exceptions to rabbinic decrees instituted in specific situations. See 5:9-10.

Kevod Shamayim - the respect for Heaven, referring to the honor of God. See 1:9, 4:3, and 5:6.

Kibbutz Galuyot - ingathering of the exiles.

Kiddush Hashem - sanctification of God's Name. A Jew is required to conduct himself in a holy manner, in such a way that his actions increase reverence for Hashem's Name. Sometimes used in reference to the forfeiting of one's life for the sake of the nation of Israel and the Torah.

Kippah - called yarmulka in Yiddish, it is a head covering worn by Jewish men as a symbol of their respect and submission to Hashem above. See 5:3.

Kohen (pl. Kohanim) - priest. Priests are traditionally considered to be descendents of Aharon, the High Priest. TheKohen is the highest of the three social groups in Judaism, the other two being Levi andYisrael. Kohanim possess special tasks and commandments particularly pertaining to the Temple service. See 20:9.

Korban (pl. korbanot) - sacrificial offering brought to Hashem in the Temple. There were many different kinds of korbanot brought for specific reasons or at particular times. HaKetoret was the offering of the incense, Korban HaTamid was the daily offering brought twice a day, in the morning and at dusk, to which the prayers ofShacharit and Minchah correspond. Others include Chatat,Asham, Minchah, Shelamim, Olah, and more. See chapter 13.

Kotel - lit. wall. Also known as HaKotel HaMa'aravi, the Western wall. Located in the Old City of Jerusalem. It was a part of the Second Temple structure. See 12:5.

Lechatchilah - Initially, before the fact, or ideally. This term refers to the ideal manner in which to perform a mitzvah, as opposed to b'dieved.

Lo titgodedu - lit. you shall not separate yourselves. A prohibition forbidding us from fragmenting the nation into divergent groups with different practices. See 21:6 and footnote 7 there.

Ma'ariv - lit. brings the evening. The evening prayer, also called Arvit. See chapter 25.

Mara d'atra - lit. the master of the area. The primary rabbi of the congregation or community who is designated to resolve halachic issues and serve as the authority on matters of Jewish law of that particular location.

Maran - lit. our master. A title given to exceptionally respected rabbis, generally used to describe those who are considered to be especially influential Torah teachers and leaders.

Masechet - tractate. A term referring to one part or volume of the Mishnah or the Talmud.

Middot - measurements; ethics, principles; manners.

Midrash - derived from the Hebrew word, darash, which means to inquire. Exegesis, exposition of scripture, or homiletic teachings, as opposed to the literal interpretation of the biblical text.

Mil - a measurement of distance equal to 2000 amot, approximately 960 meters.

Minchah - lit. gift. The afternoon prayer. Also, a sacrificial offering brought in the Temple, called the meal offering. Minchah time is divided into two parts, Minchah Gedolah (large Minchah), which begins six and a half proportional hours from the beginning of the day and lasts for nine and a half hours, and Minchah Ketanah (small Minchah), which begins nine and a half proportional hours from the beginning of the day and lasts until the end of the day. Also see Korban. See chapter 24.

Minhag (pl. minhagim) - custom or customary religious practice.

Minhag Ashkenaz - see Ashkenaz.

Minyan (pl. minyanim) - lit. quorum. An assembly of ten competent Jewish males required for the recital of certain communal prayers, such as matters of sanctity, including Barchu, Kedushah, Kaddish, and more. See chapter 2.

Mishebeirach - lit. He who blessed. A prayer for an individual's well-being, recited by the gabbai in the synagogue following the blessings on the Torah reading.

Misheyakir - lit. when one can recognize. The time of morning when light emerges, at which point it is possible to recognize a friend at a distance of four amot. This time is equivalent to the time of morning when one can distinguish between techelet (azure; light blue) and white. See 11:2 and footnote 2 there.

Mishnah (pl. Mishnayot) - from the word repetition or teaching. A compilation of Jewish oral law compiled by Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi a few generations after the destruction of the Second Temple. The Mishnah is divided into six "sedarim" ("orders" or volumes): Zeraim, Moed, Nashim, Nezikin, Kodashim, and Teharot. See Gemara.

Mitzvah (pl. mitzvot) - commandment. A religious obligation; one of the 613 positive and negative biblical commandments; loosely used to describe a good deed.

Mizmor (pl. mizmorim) - psalm or song of praise. See 14:1.

Mohel - the person who performs a brit milah (circumcision).

Muezzin - the Islamic religious official chosen from the people of the mosque to call out the time of prayers. See 24, note 4.

Musaf - lit. added. The additional prayer service recited on Shabbat, Rosh Chodesh, and festivals, corresponding to the additional sacrificial offerings brought in the Temple on those special days. See 18:9.

Ne'ilah - lit. locking or closing. The concluding Yom Kippur prayer service in which the gates of Heaven close and our future for that year is sealed by Hashem. This prayer was also recited on public fast days as a request for rain.

Nedavah - lit. donation. The voluntary prayer which corresponds to the voluntary sacrificial offerings brought in the Temple. See 18:3.

Netz HaChamah - lit. emergence of the sun. Halachic sunrise, also referred to as Netz. Regarding some halachic issues, it indicates the beginning of the morning, instead of amud hashachar. Its exact time is the subject of halachic debate. See 11:5-6.

Nusach (pl. nusachim) - wording or version. The various styles of the prayers and texts that exist in different ethnic groups, including content and order of the prayers as well as musical rendition and cantillation sounds. The main nusachim are: Nusach Ashkenaz, Eidot Mizrach, Nusach Sephard (also called Sephard-Chassidi or Nusach Ha'Ari, which was founded by the Ari, Rabbi Isaac Luria, and practiced by kabbalists, as well as adopted by someChassidim), and Nusach Teiman (the Yemenitenusach) of which there are two kinds - Baladi (a purely Yemenite nusach) and Shami (a Sephardic-like nusach). See chapter 6.

Olah - lit. that which rises. Burnt offering, brought to signify one's complete submission to God's will, as it was completely consumed by fire on the altar.

Parashah - portion or section. One of the fifty-four weekly Torah portions read in part on Monday and Thursdays and in full on Shabbat. So as to fully complete the Torah in one year, a double portion is read on particular Shabbatot. See 22:1.

Parochet - curtain. The curtain in front of the doors before the holy ark (Aron HaKodesh) in a synagogue. It is usually colorfully decorated and changed on high Holy Days as well as other festivals. See 3:6.

Parsah - measure of distance equivalent to 4 mil, or approximately 3840 meters.

Perisat Shema (or Pores al Shema) - lit. dividing the Shema. In a situation in which ten people who prayed individually subsequently assemble to form a minyan, they are permitted to respond to the recital of Kaddish, Barchu, and the first berachah of Shema - Yotzer HaMeorot. Because only the first berachah of the Shema is recited and not the complete Shema with its berachot, it is therefore considered "divided." See 19:9.

Plag HaMinchah - lit. half of the Minchah. A proportional hour and a quarter before the end of the day. See 24:7.

Poche'ach - a person whose clothes do not cover his body in a respectable manner. He is prohibited from leading the prayer service. See 4:4.

Posek (pl. poskim) - Jewish rabbis who have the authority to rule in matters of halachah.

Responsa - also called Shut (she'eilot v'teshuvot). Answers to questions concerning Torah observance posed to Jewish halachic authorities.

Revi'it - lit. quarter. A quarter of a log (a unit of volume equal to a liquid measure), equal to approx. 86 ml.

Rishon (pl. Rishonim) - lit. first one. Rishonim are earlier authorities of Talmudic law active from the period of the Geonim, from the ninth century until the sixteenth century.

Ruach hakodesh - lit. the holy spirit. Divine inspiration or prophetic insight given to those who are worthy.

Ruach Ra'ah - lit. evil spirit. There are circumstances and conditions that cause the evil spirit to rest upon a person or food. For example, the Talmud states that when we sleep an evil spirit rests upon our hands, and until we wash our hands we are not clean from it. See 8:3-4.

Sandak - the person given the honor of holding the boy being circumcised during the brit. See brit milah.

Sanhedrin - the high court and legislative body that decided halachic rulings from the period of early Judaism and into rabbinic times. Also the name of the fourth tractate in the Mishnayot of Nezikin which pertains to judicial procedure.

Sephard (pl. Sephardim) - derived from Sepharad, the Hebrew word for Spain. Jews from Spain and the Middle East, as opposed to Ashkenazim. Sephardic Jews usually pray in a Sephardic nusach (Eidot Mizrach) and practice Sephardic customs. See chapter 6.

Sephardi-Chassidi - see Nusach.

Seudah - feast or meal that includes bread. A seudat mitzvah is a celebratory feast immediately following the fulfillment of a mitzvah, such as a wedding, brit milah, bar/bat-mitzva, or a siyyum (completion of learning an important work of Jewish literature such as a tractate of Talmud). See 2:5 and 24:6.

Shacharit - the morning prayer, the first of the three daily prayers. See 11:4.

Shechinah - the Divine presence.

Shelamim - peace offering. There are many types of peace offerings, brought on the festivals and on different occasions. Parts of this offering were eaten not only by the Kohanim but also by the person who brought the offering.

Sheloshim - lit. thirty. The first thirty days of the mourning period following the death of the deceased, during which time the mourner may resume his daily routine but must continue to observe certain mourning practices. See 4:7.

Shem u'malchut - the name of Hashem and His heavenly kingdom, a phrase that must be employed in every berachah.

Shev v'al ta'aseh - to sit passively and refrain from performing an action. See chapter 14, note 9.

Sheva berachot - lit. seven blessings. The term refers to the seven benedictions recited over a cup of wine at the Jewish wedding ceremony as well as to the week following the wedding. Also called Birkot HaNisu'im (the blessings of marriage).

Shiva - lit. seven. The seven-day mourning period immediately following the burial of the deceased, which applies to cases concerning immediate relatives, such as a parent, child, sibling, or spouse. Upon conclusion of this first week, certain laws of mourning cease. See 4:7.

Shoteh - a deranged person. Because of his status, determined based on his mental capabilities, he is exempt from performing certain mitzvot. See 2:6 and 5:11.

Shtender - lectern. A stand upon which one's books and siddurim are placed and against which people lean to learn and pray.

Shut - see Responsa.

Simchah (pl. semachot) - joyous occasion which generally comes in conjunction with the fulfillment of a mitzvah. See 21:8.

Sod - lit. secret. The deeper meaning or understanding of scripture in which certain Torah concepts are elucidated.

Ta'anit - a fast day. Also called yom tzom.

Talmid chacham (pl. talmidei chachamim) - lit. wise student. Torah scholar.

Talmud - see Gemara.

Tamid - lit. constant. The daily sacrificial offering brought twice a day, once in the morning, Tamid HaBoker, and once at dusk, Tamid Shel Bein Ha'arbayim, to which the Shacharit and Minchah prayers respectively correspond. See 13:1, 5.

Tanach - An acronym for Torah, (the five books of Moses, also called Chumash), Nevi'im (the prophets), and Ketuvim (the writings), the three sections of the written Bible.

Tanna (pl. Tannaim) - lit. learner. A Jewish Sage, scholar, and teacher of the Mishnah. TheTannaic period followed the era of the zugot and directly preceded the period of the Amoraim.

Tashlumim - lit. completions. A supplementary Amidah recited immediately after the next obligatory Amidah, to make up for a missed Amidah. In certain cases and regarding specific prayers, tashlumim cannot be recited. See 18:8-9.

Techumei Shabbat - the Shabbat boundaries. The definitions of the permissible area within which one may walk on Shabbat.

Tefach (pl. tefachim) - handbreadth. A measure of length equal to the width of four thumbs.

Terumah (pl. terumot) - lit. donation. The separation of a person's produce, given to the Kohen. Upon separation, this portion attains a special sanctity. There are different kinds of terumot with different amounts. Terumot and Ma'asrot are one of the mitzvot which are dependent on the Land and can only be performed in Eretz Yisrael.

Teshuvah - lit. returning. Repentance, representing a return to Hashem after sinning.

Tikun - lit. rectification or repair. A mystical kabbalistic term used to describe the act of correcting something in this world ( Tikun Olam).

Tinok shenishba - lit. baby that was held captive. A person who was raised among gentiles and therefore does not know the laws of the Torah. As a result, his sins are deemed as due to circumstances beyond his control. See 2:8 and footnote 10 there.

Todah - thanks. The sacrificial offering of thanksgiving brought in the Temple by someone who experienced danger, as an expression of thanks to Hashem for saving him from harm.

Torah Shebichtav - the written Torah, as opposed to Torah Shebe'al Peh, (the oral Torah, traditionally passed on by word of mouth, now having come to include theTalmud, Mishnah, Beraita, and Tosefta), is the Tanach, constituting of the Chumash (Pentateuch or five books of Moshe), Nevi'im (prophets), and Ketuvim (writings).

Tzaddik (pl. tzaddikim) - righteous person. One who avoids wrongdoing and is upright, just, and true.

Tzeit HaKochavim - lit. emergence of the stars. The time when three medium stars are visible in the sky and nightfall is complete. See 25:5.

Vatikin - diligent or devoted ones. People who take great care to perform the mitzvot expeditiously, at their precise times, and in the most preferred manner. The term is commonly used to describe people who hasten to recite Shema before sunrise and pray the Amidah with sunrise, at the best time. See 11:5-6.

Vidui - confession. A part of the process of atonement whereupon a person confesses his sin and expresses feelings of regret and repentance.

Yahrtzeit (Yiddish) - lit. year-time. The anniversary date of a person's death. There are different customs dictating how to commemorate the day, such as fasting, reciting mourner's Kaddish, visiting the grave of the deceased, and lighting a candle that burns for 24 hours. See 4:6.

Yarmulka (Yiddish) - see kippah.

Yeshiva (pl. yeshivot) - lit. sitting. An institution of Torah study based on and considered a continuation of the original establishments of Torah learning in Babylonia and Israel.

Yishuv - lit. settlement. A relatively small modern-day community in Israel, as opposed to a city or town.

Yisrael (pl. Yisraelim) - Israelite, as opposed to the Kohen and Levi; called up third for the Torah reading in the synagogue. See 22:7.

Yohara - arrogance, haughtiness. There are certain enhancements of mitzvot which the rabbis prohibited lest the individual performing them be considered exhibiting yohara. See 3:7 and 17:6.

Zemirat HaKerem - lit. the pruning of the vineyard. It is from these words that the name Pesukei d'Zimrah (verses of praise) is derived. See 14:1.

Zimun - lit. invitation, summons. A ritual performed by at least three Jewish adults in which one of them invites the others to join him in reciting Birkat HaMazon (Grace After Meals).

Zt"l or zatzal - zecher tzaddik livrachah - may the memory of the righteous be a blessing. Said in reference to deceased prominent Jewish scholars and influential rabbis and teachers. Similar to zal - zichrono livrachah - of blessed memory, said about all decent Jews who have died.
